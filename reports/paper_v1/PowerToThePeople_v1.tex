\begin{abstract}
Despite popularity of playlists on streaming platforms, music genres remain a primary organizational tool among listeners, industry stakeholders, and researchers. Categorical genre assignments underpin music genre recognition in MIR and recommender systems research as well as foundational musicological research on preferences and functions. Yet `correct' genre labels for popular music and the structure of genre taxonomies are often contested, raising concerns about reliability, validity, and comparability of research results. Contrarily, popular music studies have long emphasized the listener-dependent nature of genre assignments - they may be considered as majority voting within a listener community. So why not make a virtue of necessity? We present a methodology to construct balanced hierarchical genre trees from folksonomy genre votes and demonstrate its feasability for the \textit{MusicBrainz} folksonomy and 167,762 tracks frequently listened to in Germany across the past 50 years. The resulting Community Music Genre Tree for popular music in Germany (COMGET-G) comprises 234 genres—valuable for cultural analyses, but too detailed for many applied research tasks. We address this with an algorithm to fold such trees to any desired number of categories (FOLDCAT). For tracks lacking community votes, we develop machine-learning classifiers at multiple granularity levels using openly available music features and metadata from online databases. Exemplary results indicate that our approach can assign an appropriate genre category to virtually any popular music track, representing a multiple listeners' perspective  in a transparent, reproducible, and intersubjectively comprehensible way.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Although playlist-driven listening has grown alongside the global diffusion of streaming platforms, genre labels still remain the primary way people and institutions organize popular music: Listeners search and browse by genre; playlist editors and DJs assemble genre-based playlists; A\&R, marketing and music labels position and promote artists using genre tags; and music journalists and researchers rely on genre labels to describe and compare popular music~\citep{boniniPlatformedHowStreaming2024}. While some scholars argue that classificatory practices have weakened since the late twentieth century~\citep{dimaggioClassificationArt1987} and that certain genre boundaries are unstable~\citep{silverGenreComplexesPopular2016}, empirical evidence shows that listening and social signaling remain organized around genres~\citep{airoldiFollowAlgorithmExploratory2016, lonsdaleMusicalTasteIngroup2021}. Furthermore, on most commercial music streaming platforms, genre assignments are embedded in metadata and pipeline logics—and hence used as search filters, playlist seeds, and features in recommender systems. Thus, platform algorithms amplify genres' practical significance for music discovery and market positioning~\citep{valverdeInequityDesignMusic2025}. For researchers, conversely, genre labels serve both theoretical and methodological roles: they form variables in preference and reception studies (anonymized authors, XXXX)%~\citep{lepaPopularMusicEntertainment2020}
, inputs to recommender-system research~\citep{porcaroAssessingImpactMusic2024}, and useful aggregator variables for measuring music diversity and mitigating hubness in high-dimensional audio and metadata feature spaces~\citep{flexerHubnessCaseTechnical2018,flexerProblemLimitedInterrater2016}.

However, `correct' genre labels for individual popular music phenomena (i.e., tracks, artists, releases, scenes, market, playlists, labels, performances etc.) are frequently contested~\citep{vanvenrooijCategoricalAmbiguityCultural2018}. In fact, different actors may attach different genre labels to the very same music releases. Artists employ them to performatively signify their musical identity; publishers and distributors tag releases for strategic market placement; critics and musicologists apply normative or historical taxonomies; and listeners use genre terms to organize their listening collections and negotiate their distinctive musical identity in everyday communicative interactions~\citep{frithPerformingRitesValue1996,negusMusicGenresCorporate1999}. Further, not only do chosen genre labels diverge, but the taxonomical systems  employed also differ strongly in degree of differentiation. For example, the commercial music metadata platform \textit{Gracenote} uses a compact set of 25 genre tags~\citep{jiangGenreBasedAnalysisNew2024}, whereas \textit{Spotify} exposes many thousands of genre tags~\citep{mcdonaldEveryNoiseOnce_nodate}. Music databases and platforms also differ in terms of the objects to which they assign genres (track, release, or artist) and in assignment strategies (single primary genre versus multi‑label tag sets).

These differences pose methodological challenges for empirical researchers across MIR and computational musicology. Problems encompass inconsistent genre labels that can degrade generalizability of supervised machine-learning models~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}, heterogeneously interpreted survey items in questionnaires on musical preferences  ~\citep{langeCategorizingMusicGenres2025}, and, on a larger scale, the issue of noncomparability between studies ‐ undermining replications and meta‑analyses~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. In spite of these well-known challenges, researchers in MIR and computational musicology often pragmatically draw on available commercial or open taxonomies, without systematically engaging with the actual source of labels or underlying theoretical assumptions~\citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. To address these problems, the present paper presents a theory-based, reproducible procedure to derive a scalable and transparently constructed genre taxonomy for any given corpus of popular music based on the community votes of a matching genre folksonomy. Paired with algorithmic music‑genre recognition, this approach can (1) surface empirical regularities and disagreements in genre label use that may inform popular music studies, and (2) provide a shared, probabilistic `currency' for cross‑disciplinary work in computational musicology and MIR—so that algorithmic classifiers, preference studies, and sociological analyses may operate from a common, well‑documented genre mapping.

\subsection{Musical Genres as hierarchical discourse formation}
While MIR and computational musicology often treat music genres as categories, in popular music studies they are in fact rather thought of as being organized hierarchically. For example, \cite{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000} identified genealogical links (e.g., \textit{rock} as parent of \textit{alternative rock}), geographical links (\textit{hip hop} as supergenre of \textit{German hip hop}), and historical-period links (\textit{pop} as supergenre of \textit{80s pop}) in existing taxonomies. As Brackett~(\citeyear[p. 8]{brackettCategorizingSoundGenre2016}) argues, hierarchy depth is not limited to two levels but can extend virtually indefinitely. Hence, music enthusiasts often visualize popular music genres in trees~\citep{musicmap}. However, apart from a seminal work by Schreiber (XXXX) to our knowledge, no empirical work in MIR or computational musicology has yet implemented a standardized approach to develop a genre hierarchy strictly from empirical data.

The challenge starts with the data basis: scientific consensus on which labels to select and how to properly relate them hierarchically has been seldom achieved. Alreaady expert-constructed \textit{flat} taxonomies show limited agreement~\citep{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000}, and flat music expert classifications often diverge from listener community norms~\citep{sordoQuestMusicalGenres2008}. Adopting the power-critical perspective of cultural studies~\citep{dugayDoingCulturalStudies2013}, we interpret the discourse on genres in the popular music field through Foucault's~(\citeyear{foucaultArchaeologyKnowledge2013}) concept of \textit{discourse formation}. From this viewpoint, artists, musicologists, music industry, and listeners exert unequal influence over which genre labels circulate and stabilize in the long run~\citep{lenaClassificationCultureTypes2008}. Artists often self-assign genres as strategic acts of boundary-making to gain recognition and position themselves in scenes and markets~\citep{negusMusicGenresCorporate1999}. Genres assignments by musicologists might partly mirror normative decisions rooted in struggles over cultural authority  while also serving pedagogical and archival purposes ~\citep{bourdieuMarketSymbolicGoods1985}. Finally, industry actors' genre assignments typically reflect how the music industry strategically positions and markets artists and their releases within the field which may often-times diverge from listener perception ~\citep{valverdeInequityDesignMusic2025}. We term these latter industry-driven assignments \textit{distribution genres}. By contrast, the approach of aggregating listener votes treats genre assignment as implicit majority voting within a community is privileging the perspective of those who engage with the music rather than those who distribute or normativize it. Platforms such as \mbox{\textit{RateYourMusic}}~(\citeyear{RateYourMusic_nodate}), \mbox{\textit{Discogs}}~(\citeyear{Discogs2024}), or \mbox{\textit{MusicBrainz}}~(\citeyear{MusicBrainz_nodate}) provide such crowdsourced `ground truth' on the genre of musical objects reflected - although mostly in a "flat" and not hierarchival form. On such platforms democratic aggregation replaces single-source genre assignments from label A\&R departments, artists, platform editors, or individual researchers with collective consensus, capturing how listeners actually categorize music in everyday discourse. We term resulting  listener-derived assignments \textit{community music genres}, reflecting the emergent consensus of listening communities in terms of a \textit{folksonomy} rather than the view of economically powerful institutional gatekeepers and high-authority cultural actors. 

The present paper proposes a new methodological approach that bases genre assignments on this type of listener community discourse rather than on industry, producer or expert authority. Moreover, it exploits the digital traces of the underlying democratic voting practice to also derive a bottom-up \textit{community music genre} hierarchy in tree form.  \textit{MusicBrainz} seems particularly suitable for our endeavor: it maintains a detailed whitelist of over 2,000 genre tags without imposing an a-priori hierarchy, and its democratic voting system allows users to assign and vote on tags at multiple levels (track, release, artist), and every single vote can be easily fetched from their open database. Additionally, we combine our popular music theory and ethical-driven approach of identifying listener perception of music genres identity and relationships with some important research-pragmatic considerations developed in the next section. 


\subsection{Genre taxonomy requirements for MIR and computational musicology}
When empirically deriving genre taxonomies that are aiming to be employed in future MIR research and computational musicology, several research-pragmatic requirements have to be taken into account, too. Firstly, it appears advisable to enforce balanced genre distributions in order to improve statistical power and machine learning performance~\citep{japkowiczClassImbalanceProblem2002}. However, subgenres typically receive fewer track assignments than their super categories~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}, creating an inherent imbalance propagating through hierarchical levels. A novel method to empirically derive genre taxonomies from hierarchical ground-truth should therefore optimize balance within each hierarchy level separately, constructing well-balanced tree-hierarchies where sibling genres exhibit comparable sizes regardless of their position in the hierarchy, but, if possible, without any information loss.

Second, different research questions may require different numbers of \textit{genre categories} or, as we term it, a different degree of genre \textit{granularity}. Granularity refers to the level of detail at which genres are differentiated: low granularity collapses many subgenres into few broad categories (e.g., \textit{rock}, \textit{pop}, \textit{electronic}), whereas high granularity preserves fine-grained distinctions (e.g., \textit{death metal}, \textit{synthwave}, \textit{UK garage}). Surveys of musical taste may require moderate to high granularity~\citep[e.g., 24 genres]{langeCategorizingMusicGenres2025}, machine learning models for genre classification often perform better with fewer, broader categories~\citep{genussovMusicalGenreClassification2010}, and subcultural analyses may demand very fine-grained subgenre differentiation~\citep{caparriniAutomaticSubgenreClassification2020}. Since preserving consistency with the overall hierarchical ground truth of genre assignments remains desirable, this implies a method to derive genre categories at multiple scales from a single underlying tree-hierarchy.

Third, derived genre category assignments of whatever granularity should demonstrate sufficient validity, consistency and generalizability to popular music artist, tracks and releases beyond the ground-thruth employed for constructing the category system~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. External validation may be realized by comparing genre assignments with those commonly used in MIR and computational musicology. Other aspects of validity, consistency of assignment and out-of-sample generalization may be demonstrated by training automated classifiers for the newly-created genre category system on different granularity levels drawing on openly available music features and metadata.

\subsection{Suitable feature dimensions for community music genre recognition}
Algorithmic classification of musical genres, or music genre recognition, is considered a core task in Music Information Retrieval. \cite{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024} provide a systematic state-of-the-art review and also critique how genre theory from the social sciences and humanities is unfortunately often overlooked when selecting features, often times preferring classification performance instead of theory (for a systematic review on genre theories see Merlini, \citeyear{merliniCriticalInterdisciplinarySurvey2020}). Problematically, researchers in MIR and music psychology often-times also assume that genre can be inferred from audio features alone~\citep{tzanetakisMusicalGenreClassification2002,rentfrowStructureMusicalPreferences2011}. However, this conflicts with a sociological approach to genres, which we also adopt here, describing them as ``systems of orientations, expectations and conventions that circulate between industry, text and subject''~\cite[p. 19]{nealeGenre1980}, thereby emphasizing extra-musical context alongside sound as determining factors for genre assignments. Franco Fabbri's~(\citeyear{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}) genre theory offers such a synthesis: musical style (instrumentation, rhythm, harmony, timbre) and release milieu (subcultural context, artist positioning, market placement) together inform how listeners assign genres. While MIR toolboxes extract style features from audio signals~\citep{bogdanovESSENTIAOpensourceLibrary2013},  open music databases such as MusicBrainz~\citep{MusicBrainz_nodate}, Genius~\citep{genius}, etc. also provide distributional metadata (label, chart performance, platform placement, lyrics). For our theoretical endevour that focusses on genre assignments by listeners, drawing on such metadata appears indispensible. 

Song lyrics occupy an intermediate position as feature dimension for algorithmically classifying genre, conveying both, emotional expression and stylistic content~\citep{brandCulturalEvolutionEmotional2019}, as well as contextual information on release milieu through employed language codes and topics that signify artists' subcultural affiliation~\citep{carboneStatusMarkersPopular2024}. Further, a genre classifier might need to pay special attention to the \textit{mainstream pop} or \textit{pop} metagenre that \cite{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982} describes as encompassing eclectic, commercially optimized works that resist association with a specific milieu. Artists from former niche scenes are often retrospectively relabeled as \textit{pop} or \textit{mainstream} after achieving commercial success~\citep[p. 20]{holtGenrePopularMusic2007}. Therefore, popularity itself appears to be a relevant feature dimension for classification, too. In sum, the anonymized authors (XXXX) %(~\citeyear{lepaRadke2026})
recently proposed a set of ideal-typical features for algorithmic music genre classification spanning four dimensions—musical style, release context, lyrical content, and popularity to operationalize the multi-dimensional constitution of genre following Fabbri's~(\citeyear{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}) theoretical approach. Hence, we have employed this very feature set also in the present analyis. 

\subsection{Research Questions}

Drawing on the theoretical and methodological foundations outlined above, the present work pursued three research questions: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item[] \textbf{RQ1} May we construct an empirically grounded, theoretically valid and sufficiently balanced hierarchical genre tree from multi-tag folksonomy votes for a given popular music corpus?

  \item[] \textbf{RQ2} Can we aggregate the inferred tree structure to a scalable number of distinct genre categories suitable for MIR, music recommendation and statistical applications in computational musicology? 

  \item[] \textbf{RQ3} How valid and generalizable are such community-derived hierarchical genre category assignments for empirical music research?
   \begin{itemize}
       \item[a)] To what extend do the community-derived categories agree with existing taxonomies of comparable granularity (e.g., \textit{Rosamerica}, \textit{Discogs}, \textit{Deezer})?
       \item[b)] Can algorithmic ML-classifiers based on our approach reliably predict community-derived genre categories at multiple granularities employing only openly available and theory-derivd music style, release-milieu, lyrical, and popularity features?
   \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section{RQ1: Creating a Balanced Hierarchical Genre Tree}

This section describes method and exemplary results of the novel COMGET procedure that creates a balanced hierarchical genre tree for any popular music dataset based on genre co-occurences as represented by folksonomy tags and (optionally) number of votes.

\subsection{Exemplary Data: Popular Tracks in Germany dataset (POPTRAG) and MusicBrainz Community genre tags}
As initial demonstration of our  approach, we perform the analysis depicted in this paper with a corpus of 254,334 unique popular tracks released and frequently listened to in Germany over the past 50 years (POPTRAG). Four sources comprise this corpus: 215,702 tracks from weekly \textit{MediaControl} German single and album charts (18 Nov 1977–21 Nov 2024; reduced to ~75\% where linkage success to \textit{Spotify} identifiers was possible); 26,949 \textit{Spotify} track recommendations from 126 German-market genre seeds (retrieved 29 Nov 2024; median 325 per seed, IQR 25, range 49–343); 8,801 tracks from 100 top-featured \textit{Spotify} playlists for the German market (retrieved on 29 Nov 2024, median 90 tracks per playlist, IQR 42, range 28–750); and, finally, 11,994 unique track entries from \textit{Germany's Daily Top 200 Spotify} charts (01 Jan 2017–30 Nov 2024). From all sources, we excluded tracks shorter than 30 seconds (pertains to 853 tracks, likely non-musical snippets) and retained only unique title-artist combinations, preferring earlier release dates in case of duplicates.

In a second step, we acquired \textit{MusicBrainz} track genre tags for all music tracks in the POPTRAG corpus. Hereto, only genre labels contained in the \textit{MusicBrainz} whitelist (snapshot 16 Jun 2025) were taken into account, reduced by tags referring to genres beyond popular music (\textit{non-music}, \textit{interview}, \textit{comedy}, \textit{nature sounds}, \textit{children's music}, \textit{spoken word}, \textit{standup comedy}).  In case track-level tags were missing, we employed release tags instead. If these were missing, too, we used the artist genre tags. 
For denoising, we then required any genre tag to appear with at least 50 distinct artists in the overall corpus to ensure sufficient discursive support for any genre, if not, they were discarded. Finally, we retained only tracks in the corpus exhibiting at least two community genre tags fulfilling all of these criteria, leading to a removal of 86,613 tracks (34\%) from the corpus and yielding a \textit{MusicBrainz}-tagged subset of $N = 167,721$ tracks annotated with 234 distinct music genre tags spanning release years 1924–2024 (median 2012, IQR 16 years).  Community genre assignments comprised between 1 and 22 different tags per track (median 4, IQR 3) and cast between 2 and 135 votes per track (median 6, IQR 7).  

\subsection{Methods: Hierarchical Genre Tree Construction (COMGET)}
Based on the genre-tagged and denoised POPTRAG corpus, we algorithmically built a hierarchical genre tree from \textit{MusicBrainz} tag co-occurrences, adapting and further developing a method originally presented by Schreiber~(\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015,schreiberGenreOntologyLearning2016}). Thereto, we first constructed a weighted co-occurrence network where nodes represent \textit{genre} labels and directed edges $e_{A\rightarrow B}$ quantify how strongly \textit{genre} $A$ predicts \textit{genre} $B$ on the same track. For any ordered pair $(A,B)$, we computed a vote-weighted conditional frequency:

\begin{align}\label{eq:initial_network}
e_{A\rightarrow B} = P(B \mid A)\ \times\ \frac{1}{|T_{A,B}|}\sum_{t\in T_{A,B}}
\frac{v_{t,B}}{v_{t,\text{total}}}
\end{align}

where $T_{A,B}$ denotes tracks tagged $A$ and $B$, $v_{t,B}$ the votes for $B$ on track $t$, and $v_{t,\text{total}}$ the total votes on $t$. This formula produces asymmetric edges ($e_{A\rightarrow B} \neq e_{B\rightarrow A}$) that combine directional co-occurrence with community vote strength. To illustrate: suppose 1,000 tracks carried \textit{metal} and 600 also carried \textit{rock}, giving $P(\text{\textit{rock}}|\text{\textit{metal}}) = 0.6$. If the average vote share for \textit{rock} on co-tagged tracks equalled 0.5, then $e_{\text{\textit{metal}}\rightarrow \text{\textit{rock}}}=0.6 \times 0.5 = 0.3$. Conversely, if 6,000 tracks carried \textit{rock}, 600 of those carried \textit{metal}, then $P(\text{\textit{metal}}|\text{\textit{rock}}) = 0.1$; with an average \textit{metal} vote share of 0.7 on \textit{rock} tracks, $e_{\text{\textit{rock}}\rightarrow \text{\textit{metal}}} = 0.07$. Hence, calculated directed probabilities express both prevalence and tag confidence at the same time.

In a second step, we resolved reciprocal track connections to obtain a unidirectional scaffold. For each pair of tags, we thereto subtracted the weaker directional probability from the stronger and kept only the positive residual:

\begin{align}\label{eq:remove_weaker_edge}
e'_{A\to B} &= \big(e_{A\to B}-e_{B\to A}\big)_+ \\
e'_{B\to A} &= \big(e_{B\to A}-e_{A\to B}\big)_+
\end{align}

In the example this yields $e'_{\text{\textit{metal}} \rightarrow \text{\textit{rock}}} = 0.6 - 0.07 = 0.53$ and $e'_{\text{\textit{rock}}\rightarrow \text{\textit{metal}}}= 0$, making \textit{metal} $\rightarrow$ \textit{rock} a clear candidate supergenre link. 

Third, we enforced a strict tree-like structure by assigning to each node its single strongest outgoing edge as its \textit{supergenre}; while all other edges were discarded. Components without \textit{supergenre} were attached to an artificial root node \textit{POPULAR MUSIC} to guarantee connectivity of any genre tag to the overall tree. In the example, \textit{metal} would attach to \textit{rock} unless a stronger edge existed, and \textit{rock} would attach to the root if it had no \textit{supergenre}.

\label{sec:gini_explanation}
Finally, we balanced the tree in terms of optimizing leaf-node populations on each hierarchical level. Thereto, we estimated each \textit{genre's size} as the sum of its assignment probabilities across tracks in the corpus, then computed the Gini coefficient within each hierarchical tree-level for all genre nodes contained, and defined overall tree imbalance as the weighted mean of all tree-level Ginis. We then searched for the least-imbalanced tree by iteratively removing the weakest hierarchical link of the whole tree, reattaching the resulting dangling tree to \textit{POPULAR MUSIC}, and recomputing imbalance again; we then finally selected the iteration with minimal overall tree imbalance.

\section{RQ2: Creating Genre Category Systems with Scalable Granularity}

This section describes procedure and exemplary results of the novel FOLDCAT tree folding algorithm that flexibly turns COMGET-type hierarchical genre trees into genre category systems with any desired level of granularity.

\subsection{Methods: Folding Algorithm (FOLDCAT)}
To turn any hierarchical genre tree into a scalable number of broader \textit{genre categories} suitable for given research problems,  we developed a greedy folding algorithm (FOLDCAT) with three objectives: optimal \textit{genre category} balance, a user-selectable number of \textit{genre categories}, and sufficient \textit{genre size} of each category for robust machine learning and statistical analysis. \textit{Genre size} also serves as a probabilistic estimate of expected track counts per \textit{category}, useful for downstream applications that require single-label track tags. 

To realize these objectives, the FOLDCAT algorithm iteratively processes leaf \textit{genres} beginning with those deepest in the hierarchy and with largest \textit{size} , continually moving upwards to the root. For each leaf \textit{genre},  \textit{size} is computed as the sum of vote-weighted assignment probabilities across all tracks corresponding to the leaf's genre label in the corpus. We then check whether a research-pragmatically defined \textit{size} threshold (for example t=100 for having enough exemplars in later ML classification)  is met. If it does the algorithm will keep that leaf \textit{genre} as a final \textit{genre category}. Otherwise, the algorithm absorbs the leaf \textit{genre}---along with its siblings and any descendants already processed---into its \textit{supergenre} by remapping all track assignments upward. This \textit{folding procedure} continues until all remaining \textit{genres} meet the minimum threshold.  For a desired number of \textit{genre categories}, users may then select the folding solution corresponding to a local Gini minimum, drawing on a hierarchy-level-weighted Gini coefficient (see section~\ref{sec:gini_explanation}), thereby ensuring well-balanced \textit{categories} across all hierarchy levels. A detailed flowchart of the FOLDCAT algorithm is provided in the appendix. For the present exemplary analysis using the POPTRAG corpus turned into COMGET-G, conducted a grid search across minimum \textit{genre sizes} from 1,000 to 15,000 in increments of 5 and then selected four different local Gini minima from the results for comparison.

\textit{Single-label genre categories} can then be assignmend to each track in the corpus via majority voting. Thereto, all  original genre tags are mapped to their \textit{category assignment} in the folded COMGET tree, then each track receives the \textit{category} tag with the highest summed votes. Ties are resolved by selecting the \textit{genre category} with lower total assignment probability in the corpus, thereby favoring more detailed \textit{genre category} assignments.

\section{RQ3: Demonstrating Validity, Reliability and Generalizability of Results}

Drawing on the the POPTRAG corpus of German popular music and \textit{MusicBrainz} community genre tags, this section presents empirical comparisons of track genre assignments resulting from our novel community music genre categorization approach (COMGET + FOLDCAT) with those resulting from some genre category systems frequently used in MIR and computational musicology,  Furthermore, it provides results of algorithmic genre classifier training we performed with results of the same novel community genre category systems. Both analyses were conducted with different genre granularities.

For both analyses described in this section, we derived categorigcal genre assignments for each track in the denoised and MusicBrainz-tagged POPTRAG corpus at four different levels of granularity (COMGET-G5, COMGET-G12, COMGET-G25, COMGET-G32) using COMGET and FOLDCAT as described in the previous section.

\subsubsection{Machine Learning Workflow}

In our machine learning workflow (see section B in the appendix), we first discarded tracks with more than 40\% missing values on any variable, retaining 155,093 tracks (92\%). We then performed an 80/20 train–holdout split of all observations on artist level to mitigate possible artist leakage effects~\citep{flexerEffectsAlbumArtist2010}. For training our classifiers, we employed 5-fold cross-validation (also group-split on artist level) for hyperparameter tuning. To reduce sparsity and high-cardinality effects in categorical predictors, we drew a 20\% subsample of the training set, created artist‑grouped CV folds on that subsample, and relabeled any factor level that occurred fewer than 100 times in at least one fold as ``other''. Predictors that collapsed to a single ``other''-level as well as two‑level predictors where the ``other'' level had fewer than 100 observations were removed. This preprocessing reduced the number of variables from 192 to 149. All remaining categorical predictors were dummy-coded, using the most common level as reference.

Further preprocessing was applied in sequence within each single fold: median imputation, adaptive class balancing (excluded for holdout set), and normalization. Adaptive class balancing employed a single tunable \textit{target-ratio} parameter governing rebalancing degree. The algorithm computed a reference count from outcome class frequencies (median for $\leq$ 10 classes; 60th percentile for 11–20 classes; 65th percentile for $>$ 20 classes), then downsampled classes exceeding reference $\times$ \textit{target ratio} using NearMiss~\citep{zhangKNNApproachUnbalanced2003} and upsampled classes below reference $\div$ \textit{target ratio} using Borderline SMOTE~\citep{hanBorderlineSMOTENewOverSampling2005} or regular SMOTE when sufficient danger observations were unavailable. This approach preserved more data than fixed-ratio sampling while remaining tunable: \textit{target ratio} = 1 enforced perfect balance; higher values retained original imbalance. The reference-count heuristic scaled to problem complexity, preventing excessive downsampling at higher resolutions.

For hyperparameter search in terms of this target-ratio and learner algorithm settings, we employed Bayesian optimization~\citep{snoekPracticalBayesianOptimization2012a} with a Latin hypercube initial grid ~\citep{husslageSpacefillingLatinHypercube2011}, early stopping if no improvement occurred after 10 iterations, and exploration of uncertain hyperparameter regions after 5 iterations without improvement. The final hyperparameters were selected over all tunes using the one-standard-error rule~\citep{hastieModelAssessmentSelection2009}. We tuned hyperparameters across five folds using the macro-averaged F1 score (mean per-class F1) to treat all \textit{genres} equally during lerner evaluation. 

\subsubsection{Learner Choice,  Hyperparameter Tuning and Performance Evaluation}
During initial hyperparameter tuning, we evaluated four different supervised ML algorithms based on a 20\% subsample of the training data at low (5) and medium (25) granularity to initially identify the most suitable learner out of 4 options: elastic net (GLMNET; Tay et al.,~\citeyear{tayetalElasticNet2023}), regularized discriminant analysis (RDA; Weihs et al.,~\citeyear{weihsetalKlaR2005}), random forest (RF; Wright and Ziegler,~\citeyear{wrightetalRanger2017}), and light gradient boosted machine (LightGBM; Shi et al.,~\citeyear{shietalLightgbm2025}). For each we tuned key hyperparameters: GLMNET — \textit{penalty} and \textit{mixture} in [0, 1]; RDA — \textit{covariance fraction} and \textit{identity fraction} in [0, 1]; RF — \textit{feature fraction considered at each split} in [1, \# of predictors] and \textit{leaf node size} in [1, 100]; number of trees was set to 500 for tuning and 1000 for final models; LightGBM — \textit{boosting rounds} in [50, 2000], \textit{learning rate} in [0.001, 0.1], \textit{leaf node size} in [1, 100], \textit{tree depth} in [3, 15], and \textit{feature fraction} in [1, \# of predictors]. For all learners, the target ratio used for adaptive balancing was tuned in between [1, max(obsevations per class)/min(observations per class)]. To identify the best learner, we employed a coarse hyperparameter search with a 10-level initial grid and a maximum of 25 iterations for Bayesian optimization. Higher levels of granularity (25, 32) were not considered at this initial step due to sparseness of outcome levels in the 20\% subsample. After selecting the learner with best performance accross folds, we performed a more extensive hyperparameter search with a 20-level initial grid and a maximum of 50 iterations with 5-fold crossvalidation on the full training set for all four levels of genre granularity (5, 12, 25, 32). 

Finally, we trained the tuned classifiers on the full training set, and evaluated results on the holdout set, also calculaing model-based feature importances and  confusion matrices for interpreting classification outcomes. Here, \textit{Community certainty} (overall and for single categories) served as a reference point for evaluating final classifier performance, since ML models logically cannot exceed the consistency of the underlying human community assignments~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. For this comparison, \textit{certainty} was computed as the mean assignment probability across all \textit{MusicBrainz }votes for each track that had been mapped to the \textit{single-label genre} during the FOLDCAT procedure that built the category systems. All random operations in the machine learning workflow used the seed 42 for reproducibility.

\section{Discussion}

With the present article, we introduce a novel and methodologically transparent procedure for constructing well-balanced hierarchical genre taxonomies and according genre category assignments from listener-community discourse on genres represented in folksonomies on open music databases. Specifically, we make three methodological key contributions: (RQ1) an algorithm to construct a balanced hierarchical genre tree (COMGET) from folksonomy genre tag co-occurrences, (RQ2) a folding algorithm (FOLDCAT) that generates optimally balanced hierarchical genre categories and final track-assignments at user-specified granularities from such trees. Finally, exemplarily employing a large German popular music corpus (POPTRAG) and MusicBrainz folksonomy votes, we (RQ3) demonstrate that resulting community-based genre assignments are (a) comparable in content to existing and frequently-used popular music genre taxonomies (i.e. \textit{Rosamerica, Discogs, Deezer}) while exhibiting more desirable features with a German popular music corpus in terms of category balance and category coverage and (b) can be learned well with supervised machine learning by drawing on openly available and theoretically interpretable music descriptors only. Hence, we recommend our novel approach  as a transparent, reproducible, and empirically grounded alternative to commercial or expert-curated genre taxonomies in future applied or basic research. The method can in principle be adopted to any other music corpus and other genre tagging systems that provide co-occurences of tags (e.g. \textit{Spotify}, \textit{YouTube}, \textit{SoundCloud}). Also, while we focussed our exemplary analysis on track genre, the approach can also be used for assigning genres to artists or releases in a similar way.


\subsection{Interpretation from the Perspective of Computational Musicology and Cultural Analysis}

Our COMGET analysis of the POPTRAG dataset of German popular music (RQ1) illustrates the method's capacity to computationally uncover popular music genre discourse structures. \textit{Rock}, \textit{pop}, and \textit{hip hop} emerged as three primary clusters for German popular music genres in Germany, partially aligning with \citet{silverGenreComplexesPopular2016} who identified three main international clusters analyzing MySpace artist networks in 2007. However, structures differ: \textit{rock} forms a distinct cluster in POPTRAG versus Silver et al.'s scattered "Niche" genres; \textit{hip hop} shows minimal differentiation (consistent with Silver et al.); \textit{pop} exhibits stylistic diversity versus Silver et al.'s "Rock 'n Roll" category. Direct comparison remains limited—probably since Silver et al. analyzed artists on a social networking platform, while we analyze tracks in a national listening corpus across different decades. However, our method was exactly created to allow future comparative analyses across corpora, revealing how genre discourse may vary by platform, national context, and historical period~\citep{pichlMiningCultureSpecificMusic2017,nieInaccuratePredictionGenre2022}. 

Crucially, hierarchical COMGET genre taxonomies as obtained in results of RQ1 also capture how contemporary music listeners in general categorize popular music rather than musicological historical genealogies~\citep{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000} do. While COMGET-G indeed reveals some unambiguos genealogical links (\textit{rock} $\rightarrow$ \textit{alternative rock}), we also obtained some links that seem counterintuitive at first glance. However, rather than demanding  genealogical correction from a musicological stance, these links invite for popular music discourse interpretation: For instance, \textit{classical} and \textit{jazz} emerge as \textit{pop} subgenres in the COMGET-G tree. Two factors explain this placement: POPTRAG mostly contains very accessible, popular and partly even crossover tracks from these genres—i.e., popular opera arias, smooth jazz compilations, chart-oriented classical crossover artists. This corpus profile combined with listeners' tendency to apply broad labels when tagging popular music~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019} may explain the findings. Similarly, both \textit{heavy metal} and \textit{metal} appear as peer \textit{rock} subgenres, reflecting shared historical roots in 1970s psychedelic and hard rock while acknowledging the \textit{metal's} later emerging distinctiveness~\citep{gracykHeavyMetalGenre2016}.

FOLDCAT (RQ2) enables coherent analysis of genre discourse at scalable granularities without unneccessary information loss as we have demonstrated, offering a consistent and transparent set of genre categories for studies that operate on the same corpus at different levels of detail (e.g., coarse broad genre analyses vs. fine-grained subcultural analyses on POPTRAG). This allows researchers to choose best-suitable number of categories for analyzing playlist diversity with concentration indices~\citep{porcaro20YearsPlaylists2019} or for deriving individual music taste paters~\citep{peterson1992understanding} from digital user traces on music platforms, within a framework that preserves comparability across studies through transparently derived genre categories.  Also psychological questions may be persued (e.g., linking personality traits to music listening behavior; Anderson et al.,~\citeyear{andersonJustWayYou2021}) and finally, computational analyses of music history (e.g., investigating the historical emergence and chart dominance of genres, Petibon and Hitchcock;~\citeyear{petitbon_hitchcock_2022}) from the perspective of listener discourse become possible.

From a philsophical point of view, both COMGET (RQ1) and FOLDCAT (RQ2) consequently implement power-critical concepts from popular music studies and cultural analysis~\citep{dugayDoingCulturalStudies2013} into computational methods for making them applicable with large popular music corpora. Specifically, our approach employs radically democratic principles throughout: community votes determine co-occurrences, strongest links define hierarchies, majority voting assigns single genre labels. Resulting community genre taxonomies, genre categories, and genre category assignments reflect collective listener discourse rather than industry or musicological expert impositions, democratizing genre knowledge production for computational musicology. We envision that this will improve the validity of music-preference studies by stronger aligning measurements with how listeners actually categorize music.

Lastly, feature importances obtained when developing algorithmic classifiers for the COMGET-G solutions of different granularities (RQ3) reveal the mechanisms which listener communities actually employ when attribute popular music genres: artist-level metadata dominate predictions across all granularities tested, with distribution genres and artist demographics outweighting music style attributes —a finding consistent with popular music genre theory in sociology emphasizing the role of  release milieu~\citep{nealeGenre1980}. Music stylistic attributes play a secondary role: They rather help to distinguish broad categories of music, while metadata and lyrics appear to be important for differentiation on lower hierarchical levels of genres. Last but not least, track popularity gains importance at finer resolutions as a genre signal itself~\citep{holtGenrePopularMusic2007}. Disappointingly, our but bag-of-words and lexical lyric features worked comparatively weak, pointing toward LLM-based embeddings as a promising futer direction for optimization of genre classification~\citep{prevedelloLyricsSuccessEmbedding2024}.  Confusion patterns produced by automated classification further illuminate boundary challenges when differentiating between single genre categories: e.g., \textit{rock} subgenre overlap (\textit{alternative}, \textit{pop rock}, \textit{indie}) reveals fuzzy boundaries between rock subgenres with respect to the features that were used for classification; \textit{electronic} permeability across \textit{pop}, \textit{jazz}, and \textit{indie} reflects stylistic fluidity - we interpret these as theoretically sound, because popular music scholars would probably also agree that these boundaries are hard to draw, Vice versa, the very good \textit{schlager}–\textit{hip hop} separation in our analysis demonstrate that when distinct release milieus and stylistic features are present, no problems arise. 



\subsection{Benefits of the presented approach for MIR Research and Music Recommender Applications}

Our novel method presented here advances the state of research in MIR as far as it extends Schreiber's (\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015}) approach by several improvements (RQ1): Different from that work, we ensure optimally-structured and fully-connected genre hierarchies by removing weak hierarchical links from the genre tree and introducing a container node at the root of the tree that reflects the content of the corpus. Our balancing mechanism identifies the optimal solution without using any numeric thresholds, rendering our method simple to reproduce without parameter adjustments. Thus, our open-source implementation facilitates straightforward application of the method to other corpora and tagging systems (as demonstrated in this paper with artist genre tags from \textit{Spotify} for RQ3), and also enables uncomplicated future updates of genre tree hierarchies with new data within fully automatic processing pipelines. 

Genre tree hierarchies in general, and specifically those resulting from our COMGET procedure may be very  complex, but FOLDCAT (RQ2) provides a means to computationally reduce any hierarchical genre tree to managable, well-balanced categories for downstream MIR and recommender application. Using the folding algorithm, researchers and practicioners are free to sets at an appropriate level of granularity for the task at hand and the size of their corpus without relying on constraints of available commercial or open taxonomies or resorting to arbitrary subsets ~\citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. Further, by hierarchical GINI-weighing, FOLDCAT suggests optimally class-balanced solutions within a larger range~\citep{japkowiczClassImbalanceProblem2002}, similar as a hyperparameter tuning procedure. Furthmore, full content consistency archieved by mere folding of underlying COMGET ground-truth enables to create comparable benchmarks at different levels of granularity for a target corpus similar as in a hyperparameter tuning procedure. In addition. underlying genre category assignments stay probabilistic and may be statistically treated as such, reflecting actual listener uncertainty in genre assignments.

Although the identified genre categories broadly align with other common taxonomies frequently used in MIR (RQ3), COMGET + FOLDCAT are able to uniquely capture the categories that are genuinely salient within a given corpus, thereby better reflecting the corpus structure and content in terms of category balance and coverage than using an external arbitrary system. For example, in our analysis, neither \textit{Discogs} nor \textit{Deezer} provided a sufficient amount of detail for differentiated rock subgenres, while both included categories that were actually not relevant to a corpus describing popular music in Germany. For developing recommender services and comparing diversity across heterogenous corpora, this appears to be very desirable . Also when aiming  at tailoring high-level audio descriptors for assessing music similarity \citep{flexerProblemLimitedInterrater2016} according to audience perception, this is a useful capacity. In generaly, audience orientation as provided by out approach also provides a more realistic picture of a machine-learning classifier's ability to generalize outside benchmark datasets \citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}, likely improving recommendation performance in real-world scenarios. Aligning labels with listeners' perception is also beneficial for building new music-recommendation systems \citep{deldjoo2024} and for realistically assessing diversity in recommender systems \citep{porcaroDiversityDesignMusic2021}.

Generally, our approach addresses a core methodological drawback in MIR: most genre-recognition methods to date lack explicit theoretical grounding \citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. In contrast, we operationalize genre-as-discourse \citep{foucaultArchaeologyKnowledge2013,nealeGenre1980}, treating genre assignment as an empirical question rather than an ontological given. We also provide a theoretical justification for our choice of audio features, metadata, and lyric features (anonymous authors, XXXX)
% ~\citep{lepaRadke2026}
. This connects computational methods with popular music studies and enables the MIR community to engage with humanities scholarship regarding the broader topic of cultural categorization~\citep{dimaggioClassificationArt1987}. Finally, using \textit{MusicBrainz} as the basis for COMGET and FOLDCAT opens up the possibility to assign genre categories to virtually any popular music track, combining the huge scope of \textit{MusicBrainz} and the possibilty to apply reliable classifiers on the track that were not tagged yet.



\subsection{Limitations}

Several constraints limit the interpretation and generalization of our results. The \textit{MusicBrainz} platform, launched in 2000, does not provide individual vote timestamps for genre tags~\citep{MusicBrainz_nodate}. Consequently, COMGET-G aggregates approximately 25 years of listener votes spanning a period during which perceptions of genre boundaries likely shifted gradually. This temporal aggregation may misrepresent historical genre perception, particularly for tracks released in earlier decades that received tags decades later. Timeline analysis shows that a substantial portion of votes stems from Bogdanov et al.'s~(\citeyear{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}) efforts to add genre votings to the \textit{Musicbrainz} database in 2021, sourcing the music platforms \textit{Last.fm}, \textit{beaTunes}, and \textit{Discogs}. Furthermore, the tagging community may diverge systematically from the targeted listening community-platform user demographics and expertise remain opaque; approximately 1.2 million anonymous editor accounts exist, but editor names are withheld for data privacy reasons, preventing detailed assessment of possible sociodemographic or expertise bias. 

Furthermore our tree construction procedure imposes structural constraints that may oversimplify genre relationships. Enforcing strict supergenre–subgenre hierarchies with single-supergenre topology prevents modeling genres with multiple valid supergenres, contradicting actual listener perception where genres can belong to multiple families simultaneously~\citep{schreiberGenreOntologyLearning2016}. This hierarchical enforcement does not directly model peer-genre relationships (e.g., parallel subgenres that share equal distance from their common parent), potentially obscuring horizontal genre similarities that listeners perceive as equally salient as vertical hierarchical links. However, we still decided for this approach, because otherwise, folding the tree to flat categories without logical inconsistencies would not be possible. 

Reducing probabilistic multi-tag assignments to single categorical labels for machine learning classification introduces a certain degree information loss. External validation comparisons with other taxonomies (\textit{Rosamerica}, \textit{Discogs}, \textit{Deezer}) did not account for the probabilistic nature of the underly community assignments, treating discrete category mappings as equivalent when the underlying vote distributions may reveal substantial uncertainty. Tie-breaking during label assignment favors rarer categories to preserve granular distinctions, which may introduce systematic bias toward niche genres while potentially overrepresenting subcultural boundaries that broader audiences perceive as less salient.

Genre classification performance obtained was below what is typically considered state-of-the-art in the field. Nonetheless, we deliberately drew on interpretable features and methods only, and refrained from deep learning methods since we aimed at good interpretability results. Also, given achievable performance ceilings based on empirical community certainty and taken into account that this is big data sample representing popular music of 50 years we think that performance is actually quite well. The observed strong correlations between community certainty and classifier performance validate this approach, grounding MIR performance evaluation in listener perception limits rather than arbitrary numeric accuracy targets.  

Finally, while our approach has proven fruitful for popular music in Germany generalizability to other musical contexts still remains unclear. Schreiber~(\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015}) demonstrated that results for taggers using different languages on a commercial platform show overall similarity with small but systematic differences, suggesting that cultural and linguistic contexts shape genre discourse in measurable ways. Future work should assess whether COMGET + FOLDCAT  will provide usefel across diverse cultural contexts, musical traditions, and listener communities beyond German and also Western~\citep{huangDiverseDatasetsResponsible2023} popular music.

\subsection{Future Directions}

We plan to continue our research in several complementary directions to strengthen and extend the presented framework. First, systematic comparison of COMGET-G with alternative folksonomy hierarchies—including MusicBrainz's own editorial hierarchy and Schreiber's~(\citeyear{schreiberGenreOntologyLearning2016}) solution-would be another helpful validity check. In addition, convening German popular music experts to review COMGET-G and label prototypical tracks at different granularities will generate validated reference data for musical taste studies. Further, analyzing more comprehensive music catalogues with lesser popularity bias, e.g., the Germany's National Library register of music that is released in Germany, would expand the application of our method beyond very popular music and provide genre descriptors applicable across all musical domains released in Germany. Also, replicating the pipeline on US and UK track corpora will hopefully illuminate how national listening cultures reshape genre structures and boundaries.  

Beyond academic research, we think that our approach presented here may also offer practical applied value for stakeholders in music industry. Radio programmers, playlist editors, and music librarians can gain insight into how audiences organize the collections they choose their repertoire from. A\&R teams may identify gaps between industry positioning and listener reception. Marketing departments can analyze which subcultural boundaries listeners perceive in comparison industry-imposed categories. Music journalists can start critical analyses extending from empirical listener consensus. These possible applications demonstrate how listener-centered hierarchical genre taxonomies as the one developed her may interdisciplinarily bridge scholarly analysis and industry practice.
