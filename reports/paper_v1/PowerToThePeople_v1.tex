\begin{abstract}
Despite popularity of playlists on streaming platforms, music genres remain a primary organizational tool among listeners, industry stakeholders, and researchers. Categorical genre assignments underpin music genre recognition in MIR and recommender systems research as well as foundational research on music preferences and functions. Yet `correct' genre labels for popular music and the structure of genre taxonomies are often contested, raising concerns about reliability, validity, and comparability of research results. Contrarily, popular music studies have long emphasized the listener-dependent nature of genre assignments - they may be considered as implicit majority voting results of a listener community. So why not make a virtue of necessity? We present a methodology to construct balanced hierarchical genre trees from folksonomy genre votes and demonstrate its feasability for the \textit{MusicBrainz} folksonomy and 167,762 tracks frequently listened to in Germany across the past 50 years. The resulting Community Music Genre Tree for popular music in Germany (COMGET-G) comprises 234 genres—valuable for cultural analyses, but too detailed for many applied research tasks. We address this with an algorithm that folds such trees to any desired number of categories (FOLDCAT). For tracks lacking community votes, we develop machine-learning classifiers at multiple granularity levels using openly available music features and metadata from online databases. Exemplary results indicate that our approach can assign an appropriate genre category to virtually any popular music track, representing a multiple listeners' perspective  in a transparent, reproducible, and intersubjectively comprehensible way.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Although playlist-driven listening has grown with streaming platforms, genre labels remain the primary way people and institutions organize popular music: e.g., listeners search and browse by genre, and playlist editors and DJs assemble genre-based playlists~\citep{boniniPlatformedHowStreaming2024}. While some argue classificatory practices weakened since the late twentieth century~\citep{dimaggioClassificationArt1987} and certain genre boundaries are unstable~\citep{silverGenreComplexesPopular2016}, empirical evidence shows listening and social signaling remain organized around genres~\citep{airoldiFollowAlgorithmExploratory2016, lonsdaleMusicalTasteIngroup2021}. On commercial platforms, genre assignments are embedded in metadata and pipelines—used as search filters, playlist seeds, and recommender features. Thus, platform algorithms amplify genres' role in discovery and market positioning~\citep{valverdeInequityDesignMusic2025}. For researchers, genre labels serve theoretical and methodological roles: variables in preference and reception studies (anonymized authors, XXXX) %~\citep{lepaPopularMusicEntertainment2020}
, inputs to recommender research~\citep{porcaroAssessingImpactMusic2024}, and aggregator variables for measuring diversity and mitigating hubness in high-dimensional audio and metadata spaces~\citep{flexerProblemLimitedInterrater2016, flexerHubnessCaseTechnical2018}.

However, `correct' genre labels for individual popular music phenomena (i.e., tracks, artists, releases, scenes, market, playlists, labels, performances etc.) are frequently contested:
% ~\citep{vanvenrooijCategoricalAmbiguityCultural2018}. 
the same release may be labeled ``indie rock'' by listeners, ``alternative'' by distributors, and ``post‑punk'' by critics. Further, not only do chosen genre labels diverge, but employed taxonomies also vary strongly in degree of differentiation. For example, the music database \textit{Discogs}~(\citeyear{Discogs2024}) uses a compact set of 15 high-level genre tags, whereas \textit{Spotify} exposes many thousands of genre tags~\citep{mcdonaldEveryNoiseOnce_nodate}. Music databases and platforms also differ in target objects (track, release, or artist) and assignment strategies (single primary genre versus multi‑label tag sets).

These differences pose methodological challenges for music information retrieval (MIR) and computational musicology: inconsistent labels degrade generalizability of supervised models~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}, survey items are heterogeneously interpreted~\citep{langeCategorizingMusicGenres2025}, and, on a larger scale, noncomparability between studies undermines replication and meta-analysis~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. Despite these well-known challenges, researchers often rely pragmatically on available taxonomies, without systematically engaging with the labels' actual sources or underlying theoretical assumptions~\citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. To address this, the present paper presents a theory-based, reproducible, and transparent procedure to derive a scalable genre taxonomy for any corpus of popular music using community votes of a matching genre folksonomy. Paired with algorithmic genre recognition, this approach can (1) surface empirical regularities and disagreements in label to inform popular music studies, and (2) provide a shared, probabilistic `currency' for cross‑disciplinary work in computational musicology and MIR—so classifiers, preference studies, and sociological analyses may operate from a common, well‑documented genre mapping.

\subsection{Musical Genres as hierarchical discourse formation}
While MIR and computational musicology often model genres as categories, popular-music studies more commonly treat them hierarchically. Describing existing taxonomies, \cite{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000} identified genealogical (e.g., \textit{rock} parent of \textit{alternative rock}), geographical (\textit{hip hop} supergenre of \textit{German hip hop}), and historical-period links (\textit{pop} supergenre of \textit{80s pop}). Brackett~(\citeyear[p. 8]{brackettCategorizingSoundGenre2016}) argues that hierarchy depth can extend virtually indefinitely. Hence, music enthusiasts often visualize music genres~\citep{musicmap}. However, apart from \cite{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015}, to our knowledge, no work has yet implemented a standardized approach to develop a genre hierarchy strictly from empirical data.


The data challenge is foundational: there is little scientific consensus on which labels to select or how to relate them hierarchically. Even expert-constructed \textit{flat} taxonomies show limited agreement~\citep{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000}, and expert classifications often diverge from listener community norms~\citep{sordoQuestMusicalGenres2008}. Adopting a power-critical cultural-studies lens~\citep{dugayDoingCulturalStudies2013}, we read genre discourse through Foucault's~(\citeyear{foucaultArchaeologyKnowledge2013}) notion of \textit{discourse formation}: artists, musicologists, industry, and listeners exert unequal influence on which labels stabilize over time\citep{lenaClassificationCultureTypes2008}. Artists often self-assign genres as strategic acts of boundary-making to gain recognition and position themselves in scenes and markets~\citep{negusMusicGenresCorporate1999}. Musicologists' assignments reflect normative and pedagogical aims~\citep{bourdieuMarketSymbolicGoods1985}. Finally, industry actors' genre assignments typically reflect how the music industry strategically positions and markets artists and releases within the field which may diverge from listener perception ~\citep{valverdeInequityDesignMusic2025}. We term the industry-driven labels \textit{distribution genres}. By contrast, aggregating listener votes treats genre assignment as implicit community majority judgment, privileging the perspective of those who engage with the music over distributors and cultural authorities. 


Platforms such as \mbox{\textit{RateYourMusic}}~(\citeyear{RateYourMusic_nodate}), \mbox{\textit{Discogs}}~(\citeyear{Discogs2024}), or \mbox{\textit{MusicBrainz}}~(\citeyear{MusicBrainz_nodate}) provide such crowdsourced `ground truth' on the genre of musical objects - although mostly in non-hierarchical form. This paper proposes a new methodological approach that bases genre assignments on the digital traces of the underlying democratic voting practice and derives a bottom-up \textit{community music genre} tree hierarchy. \textit{MusicBrainz} seems particularly suitable for this endevour: it maintains a whitelist of $>$2,000 genre tags, supports tag assignment and voting at multiple levels(track/release/artist), and exposes individual votes via its open database. Additionally, we combine our popular music theory and ethical-driven approach of identifying listener perception of music genres identity and relationships with some important research-pragmatic considerations developed in the next section. 

\subsection{Genre taxonomy requirements for MIR and computational musicology}
When empirically deriving genre taxonomies for future MIR and computational musicology research, several practical requirements apply: First, balanced genre distributions improve statistical power and machine learning performance~\citep{japkowiczClassImbalanceProblem2002}. However, subgenres typically have fewer track assignments than their supergenres~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}, producing imbalance across hierarchy levels. A novel method that derives taxonomies from hierarchical ground truth should therefore optimize balance within each hierarchy level, producing tree hierarchies in which peer genres have comparable sizes regardless of depth, ideally without information loss.

Second, different research questions require different numbers of \textit{flat} \textit{genre categories}, i.e., different degrees of genre \textit{granularity}. Granularity denotes the detail of distinction: low granularity merges many subgenres into broad classes (e.g., \textit{rock}, \textit{pop}, \textit{electronic}), while high granularity preserves fine distinctions (e.g., \textit{death metal}, \textit{synthwave}, \textit{UK garage}). Surveys of musical taste may need moderate to high granularity~\citep[e.g., 24 genres]{langeCategorizingMusicGenres2025}, classification models often work better with fewer, broader categories~\citep{genussovMusicalGenreClassification2010}, and subcultural analyses can demand very fine-grained subgenre splits~\citep{caparriniAutomaticSubgenreClassification2020}. Therefore, the method should extract category sets at multiple scales from a single underlying tree-hierarchy.

Third, derived category assignments at any granularity must show validity, consistency, and generalizability beyond the ground truth used to construct them~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. External validation may be realized by comparing genre assignments with those commonly used in MIR and computational musicology. Additional validity and out-of-sample generalization can be assessed by training classifiers on the new category systems at different granularities using openly available music features and metadata.

\subsection{Suitable feature dimensions for community music genre recognition}
Algorithmic music-genre classification is a core MIR task. \cite{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024} systematically review the field and critique the frequent neglect of genre theory from the social sciences and humanities when selecting features, with performance often prioritized over theory (see Merlini, \citeyear{merliniCriticalInterdisciplinarySurvey2020}, for a review of genre theories). Problematically, MIR and music psychology researchers often also assume that genre can be inferred from audio features alone~\citep{tzanetakisMusicalGenreClassification2002,rentfrowStructureMusicalPreferences2011}, which conflicts with the sociological view we adopt: genres are ``systems of orientations, expectations and conventions that circulate between industry, text and subject''~\cite[p. 19]{nealeGenre1980}, which emphasizes that extra-musical context alongside sound matters for genre assignments. Fabbri's synthesis\citeyearpar{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982} combines musical style (instrumentation, rhythm, harmony, timbre) and release milieu (subcultural context, artist positioning, market placement) as determinants of genre for listeners. While MIR toolboxes extract style features from audio signals~\citep{bogdanovESSENTIAOpensourceLibrary2013},  open music databases (e.g., \textit{MusicBrainz}\citeyearpar{MusicBrainz_nodate}, \textit{Genius}\citeyearpar{genius}) provide distributional metadata (label, chart performance, platform placement, lyrics). 


Lyrics occupy an intermediate position as feature dimension for genre recognition, conveying both, emotional expression and stylistic content~\citep{brandCulturalEvolutionEmotional2019}, and signaling release milieu via language codes and topics that signify artists' subcultural affiliation~\citep{carboneStatusMarkersPopular2024}. Attention is also needed for the \textit{mainstream pop} metagenre Fabbri describes—eclectic, commercially optimized works that defy specific milieu affiliation—since artists from niche scenes may be relabeled as \textit{pop} after commercial success~\cite[p. 20]{holtGenrePopularMusic2007}. Thus, popularity is a relevant feature dimension. Altogether, the anonymized authors (XXXX) %(~\citeyear{lepaRadke2026})
recently proposed four ideal-typical feature dimensions—musical style, release context, lyrical content, and popularity—to operationalize genre following Fabbri~\citeyearpar{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}; we use that feature set in the present analysis. 


\subsection{Research Questions}

Drawing on the above theoretical and methodological foundations, this work addressed three research questions: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item[] \textbf{RQ1} May we construct an empirically grounded, theoretically valid and sufficiently balanced hierarchical genre tree from multi-tag folksonomy votes for a given popular music corpus?

  \item[] \textbf{RQ2} Can we aggregate the inferred tree structure to a scalable number of distinct genre categories suitable for MIR, music recommendation and statistical applications in computational musicology? 

  \item[] \textbf{RQ3} How valid and generalizable are such community-derived hierarchical genre category assignments for empirical music research?
   \begin{itemize}
       \item[a)] To what extend do the community-derived categories agree with existing taxonomies of comparable granularity (e.g., \textit{Rosamerica}, \textit{Discogs}, \textit{Deezer})?
       \item[b)] Can algorithmic ML-classifiers based on our approach reliably predict community-derived genre categories at multiple granularities employing only openly available and theory-derivd music style, release-milieu, lyrical, and popularity features?
   \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section{RQ1: Creating a Balanced Hierarchical Genre Tree}

This section describes the method and exemplary results of the novel COMGET procedure, which creates a balanced hierarchical genre tree for popular music datasets using genre co-occurences from folksonomy tags and optional vote counts.

\subsection{Exemplary Data: Popular Tracks in Germany dataset (POPTRAG) and MusicBrainz Community genre tags}
For demonstration we analyzed a corpus of 254,334 unique popular tracks released and frequently listened to in Germany over the past 50 years (POPTRAG). The corpus combines four sources: 215,702 tracks from weekly \textit{MediaControl} German single and album charts (18 Nov 1977–21 Nov 2024; reduced to ~75\% where linkage to \textit{Spotify} identifiers was successfull); 26,949 \textit{Spotify} track recommendations from 126 German-market genre seeds (retrieved 29 Nov 2024; median 325 per seed, IQR 25, range 49–343); 8,801 tracks from 100 top-featured \textit{Spotify} playlists for Germany (retrieved on 29 Nov 2024, median 90 tracks per playlist, IQR 42, range 28–750); and, finally, 11,994 unique track entries from \textit{Germany's Daily Top 200 Spotify} charts (01 Jan 2017–30 Nov 2024). We excluded tracks shorter than 30 seconds (853 tracks) and kept only unique title-artist combinations, preferring earlier release dates for duplicates.

We then retrieved \textit{MusicBrainz}  genre tags for all POPTRAG tracks, using only labels from the the \textit{MusicBrainz} whitelist (snapshot 16 Jun 2025) and excluding non-popular-music tags (\textit{non-music}, \textit{interview}, \textit{comedy}, \textit{nature sounds}, \textit{children's music}, \textit{spoken word}, \textit{standup comedy}).  If track-level tags were missing we used release tags, then artist tags.
For denoising, we  only kept genre tags occurring for at least 50 distinct artists in the corpus to ensure sufficient discursive support for any genre. Finally, we only retained tracks that received at least two community votes on tags fulfilling all of these criteria. This removed 86,613 tracks (34\%), yielding a \textit{MusicBrainz}-tagged subset of $N = 167,721$ tracks annotated with 234 distinct music genre tags spanning 1924–2024 (median 2012, IQR 16 years).  Community genre assignments ranged 1-22 tags per track (median 4, IQR 3) and 2-135 votes per track (median 6, IQR 7).  

\subsection{Methods: Hierarchical Genre Tree Construction (COMGET)}
Using the genre-tagged and denoised POPTRAG corpus, we algorithmically constructed a hierarchical genre tree from \textit{MusicBrainz} tag co-occurrences, extending the method of Schreiber~(\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015}). First, we built a weighted co-occurrence network where nodes are \textit{genre} labels and directed edges $e_{A\rightarrow B}$ quantify how strongly \textit{genre} $A$ predicts \textit{genre} $B$ on the same track. For each ordered pair $(A,B)$, we computed a vote-weighted conditional frequency:


\begin{align}\label{eq:initial_network}
e_{A\rightarrow B} = P(B \mid A)\ \times\ \frac{1}{|T_{A,B}|}\sum_{t\in T_{A,B}}
\frac{v_{t,B}}{v_{t,\text{total}}}
\end{align}

with $T_{A,B}$ the tracks tagged with both $A$ and $B$, $v_{t,B}$ the votes for $B$ on track $t$, and $v_{t,\text{total}}$ the total votes on $t$. This yields asymmetric edges ($e_{A\rightarrow B} \neq e_{B\rightarrow A}$) combining directional co-occurrence and community vote strength (e.g., if 1,000 tracks have \textit{metal} and 600 also have \textit{rock}, $P(\text{\textit{rock}}|\text{\textit{metal}})=0.6$; with average \textit{rock} vote share 0.5, $e_{\text{\textit{metal}}\rightarrow\text{\textit{rock}}}=0.3$, while $e_{\text{\textit{rock}}\rightarrow\text{\textit{metal}}}$ can differ, e.g., $0.05$).


Second, to resolve reciprocal links we kept only the positive residual of the stronger direction:

\begin{align}\label{eq:remove_weaker_edge}
e'_{A\to B} &= \big(e_{A\to B}-e_{B\to A}\big)_+ \\
e'_{B\to A} &= \big(e_{B\to A}-e_{A\to B}\big)_+
\end{align}
(e.g., $e'_{\text{\textit{metal}}\to\text{\textit{rock}}}=0.6-0.05=0.55$, $e'_{\text{\textit{rock}}\to\text{\textit{metal}}}=0$), producing a unidirectional scaffold.

Third, to enforce a strict tree we assigned each node its single strongest outgoing edge as its \textit{supergenre}, discarding all other edges. Components lacking a \textit{supergenre} were attached to an artificial root, \textit{POPULAR MUSIC}, ensuring full connectivity (e.g., \textit{metal} would attach to \textit{rock} unless a stronger edge existed, and \textit{rock} would attach to the root if it had no \textit{supergenre}).

\label{sec:gini_explanation}
Finally, we balanced the tree by optimizing leaf-node populations per hierarchical level: We estimated each \textit{genre's size} as the sum of its assignment probabilities across tracks, computed the Gini coefficient per hierarchical level, and defined overall tree imbalance as the weighted mean tree-level Ginis. We then searched for the least-imbalanced tree by iteratively removing the weakest hierarchical link of the whole tree, reattaching the resulting dangling subtree to \textit{POPULAR MUSIC}, and recomputing imbalance again; finally, selected the iteration with minimal overall tree imbalance.

\section{RQ2: Creating Genre Category Systems with Scalable Granularity}

This section describes procedure and exemplary results of the novel FOLDCAT tree folding algorithm that flexibly turns COMGET-type hierarchical genre trees into genre category systems with any desired level of granularity.

\subsection{Methods: Folding Algorithm (FOLDCAT)}
To turn any hierarchical genre tree into a scalable number of broader \textit{genre categories} suitable for different research problems,  we developed a greedy folding algorithm (FOLDCAT) with three goals: optimal \textit{genre category} balance, a user-selectable number of \textit{genre categories}, and a minimum \textit{genre size} per category for robust machine-learning and statistical analysis. \textit{Genre size} is the expected track count per \textit{category} (useful for single-label tagging).

FOLDCAT iteratively processes leaf \textit{genres} beginning with those deepest in the hierarchy and with largest \textit{size}, continually moving upwards to the root. For each \textit{genre},  \textit{size} is computed as the sum of vote-weighted assignment probabilities across all tracks corresponding to the \textit{genre}. If \textit{size} meets a pragmatically-defined threshold  (e.g., t=100 for having enough exemplars in later ML classification), the leaf becomes a \textit{genre category}. Otherwise the leaf, its siblings, and any already-processed descendants are absorbed into its \textit{supergenre} and their track assignments remapped upward. This \textit{folding procedure} continues until all remaining \textit{genres} meet the minimum threshold. To obtain a desired number of \textit{genre categories}, users pick the folding solution at a local Gini minimum using a hierarchy-weighted Gini coefficient (section~\ref{sec:gini_explanation}) from solutions for different \textit{size} thresholds, ensuring balanced categories across levels. A detailed flowchart of FOLDCAT is provided in the Appendix. For the exemplar COMGET-G derived from POPTRAG, we grid-searched minimum \textit{genre sizes} from 1,000 to 15,000 (step 5) and selected four local Gini minima for comparison.

Finally, \textit{Single-label genre categories} can be assigned to tracks by majority voting: map original tags to folded \textit{category assignments}, sum votes per category for each track, and assign the highest-vote category. Ties are broken by choosing the category with lower total assignment probability in the corpus, favoring more specific categories.

\section{RQ3: Demonstrating Validity, Reliability and Generalizability of Results}

Drawing on the POPTRAG corpus of German popular music and \textit{MusicBrainz} community genre tags, this section compares track genre assignments from our novel community categorization pipeline (COMGET + FOLDCAT) with \textit{genre category} systems commonly used in MIR and computational-musicology, and reports genre-classifier training results using the same community-derived category sets. For both analyses we assigned single-label \textit{categorical genres} to each track from the denoised \textit{MusicBrainz}-tagged POPTRAG track at four granularities (COMGET-G5, COMGET-G12, COMGET-G25, COMGET-G32) using COMGET and FOLDCAT; all analyses were performed at multiple granularities.

\subsection{Methods a: External Validation Strategy}
For a discussion of content validity of our community genre taxonomy solutions, we employed contingency tables to compare our results with genre classifactions of the same tracks in existing and frequently used taxonomies with similar granularity. For this, we retrieved \textit{Rosamerica} genre lables from \textit{AcousticBrainz}~\citep{jimenez2020tensorflow}, community genre assignments from the \textit{Discogs}~(\citeyear{Discogs2024}) folksonomy, and genres assignments of the commercial music streaming platform \textit{Deezer}~(\citeyear{Deezer_nodate}) for each track using our own anonymized music-data linkage tool (XXXX).
% \textit{spotilink}~\citep{radkeSpotilink2025}.
We also computed category prevalences and overall Gini coefficients per taxonomy to compare genre balance. For clarity in figures, categories comprising $<2.5\%$ of corpus tracks were excluded from visualization.

\subsection{Methods b: Genre Classification of Unseen Tracks}
To evaluate how well an algorithmic classifier predict the COMGET-G \textit{genre categories} from FOLDCAT for unseen tracks, we trained independent classifiers for each of the four solutions. To that end, we augmented POPTRAG with 192 descriptors of \textit{musical style}, \textit{release context}, \textit{lyrical content}, and \textit{popularity} using XXXX% \textit{spotilink}

\subsubsection{Learner Choice,  Hyperparameter Tuning and Performance Evaluation}
For a detailed ML workflow see Appendix B. We initially compared four different supervised learners using a coarse hyperparameter search with 5-fold crossvalidation on a 20\% stratified subsample of the training data at low (5) and medium (12) granularity to identify the most suitable model from: elastic net (GLMNET; Tay et al.,~\citeyear{tayetalElasticNet2023}), regularized discriminant analysis (RDA; Weihs et al.,~\citeyear{weihsetalKlaR2005}), random forest (RF; Wright and Ziegler,~\citeyear{wrightetalRanger2017}), and light gradient boosted machine (LightGBM; Shi et al.,~\citeyear{shietalLightgbm2025}). For each we tuned key hyperparameters: GLMNET — \textit{penalty} and \textit{mixture} in [0, 1]; RDA — \textit{covariance fraction} and \textit{identity fraction} in [0, 1]; RF — \textit{feature fraction considered at each split} in [1, \# predictors] and \textit{leaf node size} in [1, 100]; number of trees was set to 500 for tuning and 1000 for final models; LightGBM — \textit{boosting rounds} in [50, 2000], \textit{learning rate} in [0.001, 0.1], \textit{leaf node size} in [1, 100], \textit{tree depth} in [3, 15], and \textit{feature fraction} in [1, \# predictors]. 

To adress class imbalance, we used a tunable adaptive balancing that combined down- and upsampling towards a \textit{target} that was based on class distribution. For all learners, a \textit{target ratio} was tuned in [1, max(obsevations per class)/min(observations per class)]. Higher granularities (25, 32) were excluded from initial learner selection due to sparse outcome levels in the 20\% subsample. After choosing the best learner, we ran a more extensive hyperparameter search with 5-fold crossvalidation on the full training set for all four levels of genre granularity (5, 12, 25, 32).

Finally, tuned classifiers were trained on the full training set and evaluated on the holdout set; we also calculated model-based feature importances and  confusion matrices for interpreting classification outcomes. We compared classifier performance to \textit{Community certainty} (overall and per category), since models logically cannot exceed the consistency of human assignments~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. \textit{Certainty} was computed as the mean assignment probability across all \textit{MusicBrainz} votes for each track mapped to the \textit{single-label genre} during the FOLDCAT procedure. All random operations used seed 42.

\section{Discussion}

With the present article, we introduce a novel and methodologically transparent procedure for constructing well-balanced hierarchical genre taxonomies and according genre category assignments from listener-community discourse on genres represented in folksonomies on open music databases. Specifically, we make three methodological key contributions: (RQ1) an algorithm to construct a balanced hierarchical genre tree (COMGET) from folksonomy genre tag co-occurrences, (RQ2) a folding algorithm (FOLDCAT) that generates optimally balanced hierarchical genre categories and final track-assignments at user-specified granularities from such trees. Finally, exemplarily employing a large German popular music corpus (POPTRAG) and MusicBrainz folksonomy votes, we (RQ3) demonstrate that resulting community-based genre assignments are (a) comparable in content to existing and frequently-used popular music genre taxonomies (i.e. \textit{Rosamerica, Discogs, Deezer}) while exhibiting more desirable features with a German popular music corpus in terms of category balance and category coverage and (b) can be learned well with supervised machine learning by drawing on openly available and theoretically interpretable music descriptors only. Hence, we recommend our novel approach  as a transparent, reproducible, and empirically grounded alternative to commercial or expert-curated genre taxonomies in future applied or basic research. The method can in principle be adopted to any other music corpus and other genre tagging systems that provide co-occurences of tags (e.g. \textit{Spotify}, \textit{YouTube}, \textit{SoundCloud}). Also, while we focussed our exemplary analysis on track genre, the approach can also be used for assigning genres to artists or releases in a similar way.


\subsection{Interpretation from the Perspective of Computational Musicology and Cultural Analysis}

Our COMGET analysis of the POPTRAG dataset of German popular music (RQ1) illustrates the method's capacity to computationally uncover popular music genre discourse structures. \textit{Rock}, \textit{pop}, and \textit{hip hop} emerged as three primary clusters for German popular music genres in Germany, partially aligning with \citet{silverGenreComplexesPopular2016} who identified three main international clusters analyzing MySpace artist networks in 2007. However, structures differ: \textit{rock} forms a distinct cluster in POPTRAG versus Silver et al.'s scattered "Niche" genres; \textit{hip hop} shows minimal differentiation (consistent with Silver et al.); \textit{pop} exhibits stylistic diversity versus Silver et al.'s "Rock 'n Roll" category. Direct comparison remains limited—probably since Silver et al. analyzed artists on a social networking platform, while we analyze tracks in a national listening corpus across different decades. However, our method was exactly created to allow future comparative analyses across corpora, revealing how genre discourse may vary by platform, national context, and historical period
% ~\citep{pichlMiningCultureSpecificMusic2017,nieInaccuratePredictionGenre2022}. 
~\citep{nieInaccuratePredictionGenre2022}. 

Crucially, hierarchical COMGET genre taxonomies as obtained in results of RQ1 also capture how contemporary music listeners in general categorize popular music rather than musicological historical genealogies~\citep{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000} do. While COMGET-G indeed reveals some unambiguos genealogical links (\textit{rock} $\rightarrow$ \textit{alternative rock}), we also obtained some links that seem counterintuitive at first glance. However, rather than demanding  genealogical correction from a musicological stance, these links invite for popular music discourse interpretation: For instance, \textit{classical} and \textit{jazz} emerge as \textit{pop} subgenres in the COMGET-G tree. Two factors explain this placement: POPTRAG mostly contains very accessible, popular and partly even crossover tracks from these genres—i.e., popular opera arias, smooth jazz compilations, chart-oriented classical crossover artists. This corpus profile combined with listeners' tendency to apply broad labels when tagging popular music~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019} may explain the findings. Similarly, both \textit{heavy metal} and \textit{metal} appear as peer \textit{rock} subgenres, reflecting shared historical roots in 1970s psychedelic and hard rock while acknowledging the \textit{metal's} later emerging distinctiveness~\citep{gracykHeavyMetalGenre2016}.

FOLDCAT (RQ2) enables coherent analysis of genre discourse at scalable granularities without unneccessary information loss as we have demonstrated, offering a consistent and transparent set of genre categories for studies that operate on the same corpus at different levels of detail (e.g., coarse broad genre analyses vs. fine-grained subcultural analyses on POPTRAG). This allows researchers to choose best-suitable number of categories for analyzing playlist diversity with concentration indices~\citep{porcaro20YearsPlaylists2019} or for deriving individual music taste patterns~\citep{peterson1992understanding} from digital user traces on music platforms, within a framework that preserves comparability across studies through transparently derived genre categories.  Also psychological questions may be persued (e.g., linking personality traits to music listening behavior; Anderson et al.,~\citeyear{andersonJustWayYou2021}) and finally, computational analyses of music history (e.g., investigating the historical emergence and chart dominance of genres, Petibon and Hitchcock;~\citeyear{petitbon_hitchcock_2022}) from the perspective of listener discourse become possible.

From a philsophical point of view, both COMGET (RQ1) and FOLDCAT (RQ2) consequently implement power-critical concepts from popular music studies and cultural analysis~\citep{dugayDoingCulturalStudies2013} into computational methods for making them applicable with large popular music corpora. Specifically, our approach employs radically democratic principles throughout: community votes determine co-occurrences, strongest links define hierarchies, majority voting assigns single genre labels. Resulting community genre taxonomies, genre categories, and genre category assignments reflect collective listener discourse rather than industry or musicological expert impositions, democratizing genre knowledge production for computational musicology. We envision that this will improve the validity of music-preference studies by stronger aligning measurements with how listeners actually categorize music.

Lastly, feature importances obtained when developing algorithmic classifiers for the COMGET-G solutions of different granularities (RQ3) reveal the mechanisms which listener communities actually employ when attribute popular music genres: artist-level metadata dominate predictions across all granularities tested, with distribution genres and artist demographics outweighting music style attributes —a finding consistent with popular music genre theory in sociology emphasizing the role of  release milieu~\citep{nealeGenre1980}. Music stylistic attributes play a secondary role: They rather help to distinguish broad categories of music, while metadata and lyrics appear to be important for differentiation on lower hierarchical levels of genres. Last but not least, track popularity gains importance at finer resolutions as a genre signal itself~\citep{holtGenrePopularMusic2007}. Disappointingly, our but bag-of-words and lexical lyric features worked comparatively weak, pointing toward LLM-based embeddings as a promising futer direction for optimization of genre classification~\citep{prevedelloLyricsSuccessEmbedding2024}.  Confusion patterns produced by automated classification further illuminate boundary challenges when differentiating between single genre categories: e.g., \textit{rock} subgenre overlap (\textit{alternative}, \textit{pop rock}, \textit{indie}) reveals fuzzy boundaries between rock subgenres with respect to the features that were used for classification; \textit{electronic} permeability across \textit{pop}, \textit{jazz}, and \textit{indie} reflects stylistic fluidity - we interpret these as theoretically sound, because popular music scholars would probably also agree that these boundaries are hard to draw, Vice versa, the very good \textit{schlager}–\textit{hip hop} separation in our analysis demonstrate that when distinct release milieus and stylistic features are present, no problems arise. 



\subsection{Benefits of the presented approach for MIR Research and Music Recommender Applications}

Our novel method presented here advances the state of research in MIR as far as it extends Schreiber's (\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015}) approach by several improvements (RQ1): Different from that work, we ensure optimally-structured and fully-connected genre hierarchies by removing weak hierarchical links from the genre tree and introducing a container node at the root of the tree that reflects the content of the corpus. Our balancing mechanism identifies the optimal solution without using any numeric thresholds, rendering our method simple to reproduce without parameter adjustments. Thus, our open-source implementation facilitates straightforward application of the method to other corpora and tagging systems (as demonstrated in this paper with artist genre tags from \textit{Spotify} for RQ3), and also enables uncomplicated future updates of genre tree hierarchies with new data within fully automatic processing pipelines. 

Genre tree hierarchies in general, and specifically those resulting from our COMGET procedure may be very  complex, but FOLDCAT (RQ2) provides a means to computationally reduce any hierarchical genre tree to managable, well-balanced categories for downstream MIR and recommender application. Using the folding algorithm, researchers and practicioners are free to sets at an appropriate level of granularity for the task at hand and the size of their corpus without relying on constraints of available commercial or open taxonomies or resorting to arbitrary subsets ~\citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. Further, by hierarchical GINI-weighing, FOLDCAT suggests optimally class-balanced solutions within a larger range~\citep{japkowiczClassImbalanceProblem2002}, similar as a hyperparameter tuning procedure. Furthmore, full content consistency archieved by mere folding of underlying COMGET ground-truth enables to create comparable benchmarks at different levels of granularity for a target corpus similar as in a hyperparameter tuning procedure. In addition. underlying genre category assignments stay probabilistic and may be statistically treated as such, reflecting actual listener uncertainty in genre assignments.

Although the identified genre categories broadly align with other common taxonomies frequently used in MIR (RQ3), COMGET + FOLDCAT are able to uniquely capture the categories that are genuinely salient within a given corpus, thereby better reflecting the corpus structure and content in terms of category balance and coverage than using an external arbitrary system. For example, in our analysis, neither \textit{Discogs} nor \textit{Deezer} provided a sufficient amount of detail for differentiated rock subgenres, while both included categories that were actually not relevant to a corpus describing popular music in Germany. For developing recommender services and comparing diversity across heterogenous corpora, this appears to be very desirable . Also when aiming  at tailoring high-level audio descriptors for assessing music similarity \citep{flexerProblemLimitedInterrater2016} according to audience perception, this is a useful capacity. In generaly, audience orientation as provided by out approach also provides a more realistic picture of a machine-learning classifier's ability to generalize outside benchmark datasets \citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}, likely improving recommendation performance in real-world scenarios. Aligning labels with listeners' perception is also beneficial for building new music-recommendation systems \citep{deldjoo2024} and for realistically assessing diversity in recommender systems \citep{porcaroDiversityDesignMusic2021}.

Generally, our approach addresses a core methodological drawback in MIR: most genre-recognition methods to date lack explicit theoretical grounding \citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. In contrast, we operationalize genre-as-discourse \citep{foucaultArchaeologyKnowledge2013,nealeGenre1980}, treating genre assignment as an empirical question rather than an ontological given. We also provide a theoretical justification for our choice of audio features, metadata, and lyric features (anonymous authors, XXXX)
% ~\citep{lepaRadke2026}
. This connects computational methods with popular music studies and enables the MIR community to engage with humanities scholarship regarding the broader topic of cultural categorization~\citep{dimaggioClassificationArt1987}. Finally, using \textit{MusicBrainz} as the basis for COMGET and FOLDCAT opens up the possibility to assign genre categories to virtually any popular music track, combining the huge scope of \textit{MusicBrainz} and the possibilty to apply reliable classifiers on the track that were not tagged yet.



\subsection{Limitations}

Several constraints limit the interpretation and generalization of our results. The \textit{MusicBrainz} platform, launched in 2000, does not provide individual vote timestamps for genre tags~\citep{MusicBrainz_nodate}. Consequently, COMGET-G aggregates approximately 25 years of listener votes spanning a period during which perceptions of genre boundaries likely shifted gradually. This temporal aggregation may misrepresent historical genre perception, particularly for tracks released in earlier decades that received tags decades later. Timeline analysis shows that a substantial portion of votes stems from Bogdanov et al.'s~(\citeyear{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}) efforts to add genre votings to the \textit{Musicbrainz} database in 2021, sourcing the music platforms \textit{Last.fm}, \textit{beaTunes}, and \textit{Discogs}. Furthermore, the tagging community may diverge systematically from the targeted listening community-platform user demographics and expertise remain opaque; approximately 1.2 million anonymous editor accounts exist, but editor names are withheld for data privacy reasons, preventing detailed assessment of possible sociodemographic or expertise bias. 

Furthermore our tree construction procedure imposes structural constraints that may oversimplify genre relationships. Enforcing strict supergenre–subgenre hierarchies with single-supergenre topology prevents modeling genres with multiple valid supergenres, contradicting actual listener perception where genres can belong to multiple families simultaneously~\citep{schreiberGenreOntologyLearning2016}. This hierarchical enforcement does not directly model peer-genre relationships (e.g., parallel subgenres that share equal distance from their common parent), potentially obscuring horizontal genre similarities that listeners perceive as equally salient as vertical hierarchical links. However, we still decided for this approach, because otherwise, folding the tree to flat categories without logical inconsistencies would not be possible. 

Reducing probabilistic multi-tag assignments to single categorical labels for machine learning classification introduces a certain degree information loss. External validation comparisons with other taxonomies (\textit{Rosamerica}, \textit{Discogs}, \textit{Deezer}) did not account for the probabilistic nature of the underly community assignments, treating discrete category mappings as equivalent when the underlying vote distributions may reveal substantial uncertainty. Tie-breaking during label assignment favors rarer categories to preserve granular distinctions, which may introduce systematic bias toward niche genres while potentially overrepresenting subcultural boundaries that broader audiences perceive as less salient.

Genre classification performance obtained was below what is typically considered state-of-the-art in the field. Nonetheless, we deliberately drew on interpretable features and methods only, and refrained from deep learning methods since we aimed at good interpretability results. Also, given achievable performance ceilings based on empirical community certainty and taken into account that this is big data sample representing popular music of 50 years we think that performance is actually quite well. The observed strong correlations between community certainty and classifier performance validate this approach, grounding MIR performance evaluation in listener perception limits rather than arbitrary numeric accuracy targets.  

Finally, while our approach has proven fruitful for popular music in Germany generalizability to other musical contexts still remains unclear. Schreiber~(\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015}) demonstrated that results for taggers using different languages on a commercial platform show overall similarity with small but systematic differences, suggesting that cultural and linguistic contexts shape genre discourse in measurable ways. Future work should assess whether COMGET + FOLDCAT  will provide usefel across diverse cultural contexts, musical traditions, and listener communities beyond German and also Western
% ~\citep{huangDiverseDatasetsResponsible2023} 
popular music.

\subsection{Future Directions}

We plan to continue our research in several complementary directions to strengthen and extend the presented framework. First, systematic comparison of COMGET-G with alternative folksonomy hierarchies—including MusicBrainz's own editorial hierarchy and Schreiber's~(\citeyear{schreiberGenreOntologyLearning2016}) solution-would be another helpful validity check. In addition, convening German popular music experts to review COMGET-G and label prototypical tracks at different granularities will generate validated reference data for musical taste studies. Further, analyzing more comprehensive music catalogues with lesser popularity bias, e.g., the Germany's National Library register of music that is released in Germany, would expand the application of our method beyond very popular music and provide genre descriptors applicable across all musical domains released in Germany. Also, replicating the pipeline on US and UK track corpora will hopefully illuminate how national listening cultures reshape genre structures and boundaries.  

Beyond academic research, we think that our approach presented here may also offer practical applied value for stakeholders in music industry. Radio programmers, playlist editors, and music librarians can gain insight into how audiences organize the collections they choose their repertoire from. A\&R teams may identify gaps between industry positioning and listener reception. Marketing departments can analyze which subcultural boundaries listeners perceive in comparison industry-imposed categories. Music journalists can start critical analyses extending from empirical listener consensus. These possible applications demonstrate how listener-centered hierarchical genre taxonomies as the one developed her may interdisciplinarily bridge scholarly analysis and industry practice.
