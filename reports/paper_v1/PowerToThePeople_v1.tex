\begin{abstract}
Despite popularity of playlists on streaming platforms, music genres still remain a primary organizational tool among listeners, stakeholders, and researchers. Categorical genre assignments form the basis of many music genre recognition models in MIR and recommender systems research as well as in musicological basic research on music preferences and functions. Yet, `correct' genre labels for popular music exemplars and structure of genre taxonomies are often contested, raising concerns about reliability, validity, and comparability of research results. In contrast, popular music studies have long emphasized the listener-dependent nature of genre assignments. In other words: Genre assignment may be considered a matter of majority voting within a listener community. So why not make a virtue of necessity? With the present contribution, we present a new methodology for constructing hierarchical genre trees from \textit{MusicBrainz} folksonomy genre votes and demonstrate feasability of this approach  for 167,762 tracks listened to in Germany across the past 50 years. The resulting Community Music Genre Tree for popular music in Germany (COMGET-G) comprises 234 genres—interesting for cultural analyses, but probably too detailed for many applied research tasks. We address this by presenting an additional algorithm for folding such trees to a desired number of genre categories for any research problem (FOLDCAT). For automatic category assignment of music tracks still lacking community votes, we also develop ML classifiers at multiple granularity levels using openly-available music features and metadata from different online databases. Exemplary results indicate that our approach is able to assign a proper genre category representing a multiple listeners' perspective to any popular music track in a transparent, reproducible, and intersubjectively comprehensible way.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Although playlist-driven listening has grown alongside the global diffusion of streaming platforms, genre labels still remain the primary way people and institutions organize popular music: Listeners search and browse by genre; playlist editors and DJs assemble genre-based playlists; A\&R, marketing and music labels position and promote artists using genre tags; and music journalists and researchers rely on genre labels to describe and compare popular music~\citep{boniniPlatformedHowStreaming2024}. Some scholars argue that classificatory practices have weakened since the late twentieth century~\citep{dimaggioClassificationArt1987} and that certain genre boundaries are unstable~\citep{silverGenreComplexesPopular2016}, but empirical evidence shows that listening and social signaling remain organized around genres~\citep{airoldiFollowAlgorithmExploratory2016, lonsdaleMusicalTasteIngroup2021}. On most music streaming platforms, genre assignments are embedded in metadata and pipeline logics—and hence used as search filters, playlist seeds, and features in recommender systems. Thus, platform algorithms amplify genres' practical significance for discovery and market positioning~\citep{valverdeInequityDesignMusic2025}. For researchers, conversely, genre labels serve both theoretical and methodological roles: they form variables in preference and reception studies (anonymized authors, XXXX)%~\citep{lepaPopularMusicEntertainment2020}
, inputs to recommender-system research~\citep{porcaroAssessingImpactMusic2024}, and useful aggregator variables for measuring music diversity and mitigating hubness in high-dimensional audio and metadata feature spaces~\citep{flexerHubnessCaseTechnical2018,flexerProblemLimitedInterrater2016}.

However, `correct' genre labels for individual popular music phenomena (i.e., tracks, artists, releases, scenes, market, playlists, labels, performances etc.) are frequently contested~\citep{vanvenrooijCategoricalAmbiguityCultural2018}. Different actors may attach different genre labels to the very same music releases. Artists  employ them to signify their musical identity; publishers and distributors tag releases for strategic market placement; critics and musicologists apply normative or historical taxonomies; and listeners use genre terms to organize their listening collections and to negotiate their distinctive musical identity in everyday communicative interactions~\citep{frithPerformingRitesValue1996,negusMusicGenresCorporate1999}. In result, not only do chosen genre labels diverge, but the taxonomical systems  employed also differ strongly in degree of differentiation. For example, the commercial music metadata platform \textit{Gracenote} uses a compact set of 25 genre tags~\citep{jiangGenreBasedAnalysisNew2024}, whereas \textit{Spotify} exposes many thousands of genre tags~\citep{mcdonaldEveryNoiseOnce_nodate}. Music databases and platforms also differ in terms of the objects to which they assign genres (track, release, or artist) and in assignment strategies (single primary genre versus multi‑label tag sets).

These differences pose methodological challenges for empirical researchers across MIR and computational musicology. Problems encompass inconsistent genre labels that can degrade generalizability of supervised machine-learning models~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}, heterogeneously interpreted survey items in questionnaires on musical preferences  ~\citep{langeCategorizingMusicGenres2025}, and, on a larger scale, the issue of noncomparability between studies ‐ undermining replication and meta‑analysis~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. In spite of these well-known challengers, researchers in MIR and computational musicology often pragmatically adopt available commercial or open taxonomies, without systematically engaging with the actual source of labels or underlying theoretical assumptions~\citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. To address these problems, the present paper presents a theory-based, reproducible procedure to derive a scalable and transparently constructed genre taxonomy for any given corpus of popular music based on the community votes of a genre folksonomy. Paired with algorithmic music‑genre recognition, this approach can (1) surface empirical regularities and disagreements in genre label use that may inform popular music studies, and (2) provide a shared, probabilistic `currency' for cross‑disciplinary work in computational musicology and MIR—so that algorithmic classifiers, preference studies, and sociological analyses may operate from a common, well‑documented mapping.

\subsection{Musical Genres as hierarchical discourse formation}
While MIR and computational musicology often treat music genres as categories, in popular music studies they are in fact thought of as organized hierarchically. For example, \cite{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000} identified genealogical links (e.g., \textit{rock} as parent of \textit{alternative rock}), geographical links (\textit{hip hop} as supergenre of \textit{German hip hop}), and historical-period links (\textit{pop} as supergenre of \textit{80s pop}) in existing taxonomies. As Brackett~(\citeyear[p. 8]{brackettCategorizingSoundGenre2016}) argues, hierarchy depth is not limited to two levels but can extend virtually indefinitely. Hence, popular music genres are often visualized in trees~\citep{musicmap}. 

However, scientific consensus on which labels to select and how to relate them is seldom achieved. Even expert-constructed taxonomies show limited agreement~\citep{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000}, and expert classifications often diverge from listener community norms~\citep{sordoQuestMusicalGenres2008}. Adopting the power-critical perspective of cultural studies~\citep{dugayDoingCulturalStudies2013}, we interpret the discourse on genres in the popular music field through Foucault's~(\citeyear{foucaultArchaeologyKnowledge2013}) concept of \textit{discourse formation}. From this viewpoint, artists, musicologists, industry, and listeners exert unequal influence over which genre labels circulate and stabilize in the long run~\citep{lenaClassificationCultureTypes2008}. Artists often self-assign genres as strategic acts of boundary-making to gain recognition and position themselves in scenes and markets~\citep{negusMusicGenresCorporate1999}. Genres assignments by musicologists might partly mirror normative decisions rooted in struggles over cultural authority  while also serving pedagogical and archival purposes ~\citep{bourdieuMarketSymbolicGoods1985}.

Industry actors' genre assignments mainly reflect how the music industry strategically positions and markets artists and their releases within the field which may often-times diverge from listener perception ~\citep{valverdeInequityDesignMusic2025}. We term these industry-driven assignments \textit{distribution genres}. By contrast, aggregating listener votes treats genre assignment as implicit majority voting within a community, privileging the perspective of those who engage with the music rather than those who distribute or normativize it. This democratic aggregation replaces single-source genre assignments from label A\&R departments, artists, platform editors, or individual researchers with collective consensus, capturing how listeners actually categorize music in everyday discourse. We term these listener-derived assignments \textit{community music genres}, reflecting the emergent consensus of listening communities rather than the view of economically powerful institutional gatekeepers and high-authoriy cultural actors.

To empirically ground genre assignments in listener discourse rather than in industry, producer or musicological authority, we propose a new methodological approach that derives a \textit{community music genre} hierarchy and \textit{community genre} assignments for music tracks from the votings of global music listener communities, also termed \textit{folksonomies}. Platforms such as \mbox{\textit{RateYourMusic}}~(\citeyear{RateYourMusic_nodate}), \mbox{\textit{Discogs}}~(\citeyear{Discogs2024}), or \mbox{\textit{MusicBrainz}}~(\citeyear{MusicBrainz_nodate}) provide crowdsourced `ground truth' on the genre of musical objects reflected in aggregated listener tagging behavior. \textit{MusicBrainz} seems particularly suitable for our endeavor: it maintains a detailed whitelist of over 2,000 genre tags without imposing an a-priori hierarchy, and its democratic voting system allows users to assign and vote on tags at multiple levels (track, release, artist), enabling nuanced analysis of consensus and disagreement.


\subsection{Genre taxonomy requirements for MIR and computational musicology}
For the application of genre taxonomies in MIR research and computational musicology, several research-pragmatic requirements have to be taken into account. First, balanced genre distributions improve statistical power and machine learning performance~\citep{japkowiczClassImbalanceProblem2002}. However, subgenres typically receive fewer track assignments than their super categories~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}, creating an inherent imbalance propagating through hierarchical levels. A novel method to empirically derive genre taxonomies from hierarchical ground-truth should therefore optimize balance within each hierarchy level separately, constructing well-balanced tree-hierarchies where sibling genres exhibit comparable sizes regardless of their position in the hierarchy.

Second, different research questions may require different numbers of \textit{genre categories} or, as we term it, a different degree of genre \textit{granularity}. Granularity refers to the level of detail at which genres are differentiated: low granularity collapses many subgenres into few broad categories (e.g., \textit{rock}, \textit{pop}, \textit{electronic}), whereas high granularity preserves fine-grained distinctions (e.g., \textit{death metal}, \textit{synthwave}, \textit{UK garage}). Surveys of musical taste may require moderate to high granularity~\citep[e.g., 24 genres]{langeCategorizingMusicGenres2025}, machine learning models for genre classification often perform better with fewer, broader categories~\citep{genussovMusicalGenreClassification2010}, and subcultural analyses may demand very fine-grained subgenre differentiation~\citep{caparriniAutomaticSubgenreClassification2020}. Since preserving consistency with the overall hierarchical ground truth of genre assignments remains desirable, this implies a method to derive genre categories at multiple scales from a single underlying tree-hierarchy.

Third, derived genre category assignments of whatever granularity should demonstrate sufficient validity, consistency and generalizability to popular music artist, tracks and releases beyond the ground-thruth employed for constructing the category system~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. External validation may be realized by comparing genre assignments with those commonly used in MIR and computational musicology. Other aspects of validity, consistency of assignment and out-of-sample generalization may be demonstrated by training automated classifiers for the newly-created genre category system on different granularity levels drawing on openly available music features and metadata.

\subsection{Feature space for community music genre recognition}
Algorithmic classification of musical genres, or music genre recognition, is considered a core task in Music Information Retrieval. \cite{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024} provide a systematic state-of-the-art review and also critique how genre theory from the social sciences and humanities is unfortunately often overlooked when selecting features (for a systematic review on genre theories see Merlini, \citeyear{merliniCriticalInterdisciplinarySurvey2020}). Problematically, researchers in MIR and music psychology often-times assume that genre can be inferred from audio features alone~\citep{tzanetakisMusicalGenreClassification2002,rentfrowStructureMusicalPreferences2011}. However, this conflicts with a sociological approach to genres, which we also adopt here, describing them as ``systems of orientations, expectations and conventions that circulate between industry, text and subject''~\cite[p. 19]{nealeGenre1980}, emphasizing extra-musical context alongside sound as determining factors for genre assignments. Franco Fabbri's~(\citeyear{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}) genre theory offers such a synthesis: musical style (instrumentation, rhythm, harmony, timbre) and release milieu (subcultural context, artist positioning, market placement) together inform how listeners assign genres. While MIR toolboxes extract style features from audio signals~\citep{bogdanovESSENTIAOpensourceLibrary2013},  open music databases such as MusicBrainz~\citep{MusicBrainz_nodate}, Genius~\citep{genius}, etc. provide distributional metadata (label, chart performance, platform placement, lyrics). For our theoretical endevour that focusses on genre assignments by listeners, drawing on such metadata appears indispensible. 

Song lyrics occupy an intermediate position, conveying both, emotional expression and stylistic content~\citep{brandCulturalEvolutionEmotional2019}, as well as contextual information on release milieu through employed language codes and topics that signify artists' subcultural affiliation~\citep{carboneStatusMarkersPopular2024}. Further, a genre classifier might need to pay special attention to the \textit{mainstream pop} or \textit{pop} metagenre that \cite{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982} describes as encompassing eclectic, commercially optimized works that resist association with a specific milieu. Artists from former niche scenes are often retrospectively relabeled as \textit{pop} or \textit{mainstream} after achieving commercial success~\citep[p. 20]{holtGenrePopularMusic2007}. Therefore, popularity itself appears to be a relevant feature dimension for classification. In sum, the anonymized authors (XXXX) %(~\citeyear{lepaRadke2026})
recently proposed a feature space for algorithmic music genre classification spanning four dimensions—musical style, release context, lyrical content, and popularity to operationalize the multi-dimensional constitution of genre following Fabbri's~(\citeyear{fabbriTheoryMusicalGenres1982}) approach.

\subsection{Research Questions}

Drawing on the theoretical and methodological foundations outlined above, the present work pursued three research questions: 
\begin{itemize}
  \item[] \textbf{RQ1} Can we construct an empirically grounded and sufficiently balanced hierarchical genre tree from multi-tag folksonomy votes for a given popular music corpus?

  \item[] \textbf{RQ2} Can we aggregate the inferred tree structure to a scalable number of distinct genre categories suitable for machine learning and statistical applications? 

  \item[] \textbf{RQ3} How valid and generalizable are community-derived genre category assignments for empirical music research?
   \begin{itemize}
       \item[a)] To what extend do the community-derived categories agree with existing taxonomies of comparable granularity (e.g., Rosamerica, Discogs, Deezer)?
       \item[b)] Can algorithmic ML-classifiers reliably predict community-derived genre categories at multiple granularities employing openly available music style, release-milieu, lyrical, and popularity features?
   \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

In the following sections, we will hence introduce a method to create a balanced hierarchical genre tree based on genre co-occurrences in folksonomy tags (COMGET), describe a tree folding algorithm to flexibly create genre category systems with different levels of granularity for various research tasks (FOLDCAT), present comparisons of genre assignments with existing taxonomies and results of algorithmic classifier training for different granularities, and finally discuss our findings and summarize results.



\section{RQ1: Creating a Balanced Hierarchical Genre Tree}

This section describes method and exemplary results of the novel COMGET procedure that creates a balanced hierarchical genre tree for any popular music dataset based on genre co-occurences in folksonomy tags.

\subsection{Exemplary Data: Popular Tracks in Germany dataset (POPTRAG) and MusicBrainz Community genre tags}
As initial demonstration of our new methodological approach, we perform the analysis depicted in this paper with a corpus of 254,334 unique popular tracks released and frequently listened to in Germany over the past 50 years (POPTRAG). Four sources comprise this corpus: 215,702 tracks from weekly \textit{MediaControl} German single and album charts (18 Nov 1977–21 Nov 2024; reduced to ~75\% where linkage success to \textit{Spotify} identifiers was possible); 26,949 \textit{Spotify} track recommendations from 126 German-market genre seeds (retrieved 29 Nov 2024; median 325 per seed, IQR 25, range 49–343); 8,801 tracks from 100 top-featured \textit{Spotify} playlists for the German market (retrieved on 29 Nov 2024, median 90 tracks per playlist, IQR 42, range 28–750); and, finally, 11,994 unique track entries from \textit{Germany's Daily Top 200 Spotify} charts (01 Jan 2017–30 Nov 2024). From all sources, we excluded tracks shorter than 30 seconds (pertains to 853 tracks, likely non-musical snippets) and retained only unique title-artist combinations, preferring earlier release dates in case of duplicates.

In a second step, we acquired \textit{MusicBrainz} track genre tags for all music tracks in the POPTRAG corpus. Hereto, only genre labels contained in the \textit{MusicBrainz} whitelist (snapshot 16 Jun 2025) were taken into account, reduced by tags referring to genres beyond popular music (\textit{non-music}, \textit{interview}, \textit{comedy}, \textit{nature sounds}, \textit{children's music}, \textit{spoken word}, \textit{standup comedy}).  In case track-level tags were missing, we employed release tags instead. If these were missing, too, we used the artist genre tags. 
For denoising, we then required any genre tag to appear with at least 50 distinct artists in the overall corpus to ensure sufficient discursive support for any genre, if not, they were discarded. Finally, we retained only tracks in the corpus exhibiting at least two community genre tags fulfilling all of these criteria, leading to a removal of 86,613 tracks (34\%) from the corpus and yielding a \textit{MusicBrainz}-tagged subset of $N = 167,721$ tracks annotated with 234 distinct music genre tags spanning release years 1924–2024 (median 2012, IQR 16 years).  Community genre assignments comprised between 1 and 22 different tags per track (median 4, IQR 3) and cast between 2 and 135 votes per track (median 6, IQR 7).  

\subsection{Methods: Hierarchical Genre Tree Construction (COMGET)}
Based on the genre-tagged and denoised POPTRAG corpus, we algorithmically built a hierarchical genre tree from \textit{MusicBrainz} tag co-occurrences, adapting and further developing a method originally presented by Schreiber~(\citeyear{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015,schreiberGenreOntologyLearning2016}). Thereto, we first constructed a weighted co-occurrence network where nodes represent \textit{genre} labels and directed edges $e_{A\rightarrow B}$ quantify how strongly \textit{genre} $A$ predicts \textit{genre} $B$ on the same track. For any ordered pair $(A,B)$, we computed a vote-weighted conditional frequency:

\begin{align}\label{eq:initial_network}
e_{A\rightarrow B} = P(B \mid A)\ \times\ \frac{1}{|T_{A,B}|}\sum_{t\in T_{A,B}}
\frac{v_{t,B}}{v_{t,\text{total}}}
\end{align}

where $T_{A,B}$ denotes tracks tagged $A$ and $B$, $v_{t,B}$ the votes for $B$ on track $t$, and $v_{t,\text{total}}$ the total votes on $t$. This formula produces asymmetric edges ($e_{A\rightarrow B} \neq e_{B\rightarrow A}$) that combine directional co-occurrence with community vote strength. To illustrate: suppose 1,000 tracks carried \textit{metal} and 600 also carried\textit{rock}, giving $P(\text{\textit{rock}}|\text{\textit{metal}}) = 0.6$. If the average vote share for \textit{rock} on co-tagged tracks equalled 0.5, then $e_{\text{\textit{metal}}\rightarrow \text{\textit{rock}}}=0.6 \times 0.5 = 0.3$. Conversely, if 6,000 tracks carried \textit{rock}, 600 of those carried \textit{metal}, then $P(\text{\textit{metal}}|\text{\textit{rock}}) = 0.1$; with an average \textit{metal} vote share of 0.7 on \textit{rock} tracks, $e_{\text{\textit{rock}}\rightarrow \text{\textit{metal}}} = 0.07$. Hence, calculated directeid probabilities express both prevalence and tag confidence at the same time.

In a second step, we resolved reciprocal track connections to obtain a unidirectional scaffold. For each pair of tags, we thereto subtracted the weaker directional probability from the stronger and kept only the positive residual:

\begin{align}\label{eq:remove_weaker_edge}
e'_{A\to B} &= \big(e_{A\to B}-e_{B\to A}\big)_+ \\
e'_{B\to A} &= \big(e_{B\to A}-e_{A\to B}\big)_+
\end{align}

In the example this yields $e'_{\text{\textit{metal}} \rightarrow \text{\textit{rock}}} = 0.6 - 0.07 = 0.53$ and $e'_{\text{\textit{rock}}\rightarrow \text{\textit{metal}}}= 0$, making \textit{metal} $\rightarrow$ \textit{rock} a clear candidate supergenre link. 

Third, we enforced a strict tree-like structure by assigning to each node its single strongest outgoing edge as its \textit{supergenre}; while all other edges were discarded. Components without \textit{supergenre} were attached to an artificial root node \textit{POPULAR MUSIC} to guarantee connectivity of any genre tag to the overall tree. In the example, \textit{metal} would attach to \textit{rock} unless a stronger edge existed, and \textit{rock} would attach to the root if it had no \textit{supergenre}.

\label{sec:gini_explanation}
Finally, we balanced the tree in terms of optimizing leaf-node populations on each hierarchical level. Thereto, we estimated each genre node's size as the sum of its assignment probabilities across tracks in the corpus, then computed the Gini coefficient within each hierarchical tree-level for all genre nodes contained, and defined overall tree imbalance as the weighted mean of all tree-level Ginis. We then searched for the least-imbalanced tree by iteratively removing the weakest hierarchical link of the whole tree, reattaching the resulting dangling tree to \textit{POPULAR MUSIC}, and recomputing imbalance again; we then finally selected the iteration with minimal overall tree imbalance.

\section{RQ2: Creating Genre Category Systems with Scalable Granularity}

This section describes procedure and exemplary results of the novel FOLDCAT tree folding algorithm that flexibly turns COMGET-type hierarchical genre trees into genre category systems with desired level of granularity.

\subsection{Methods: Folding Algorithm (FOLDCAT)}
To turn any hierarchical genre tree into a scalable number of broader \textit{genre categories} suitable for given research problems and machine learning applications, we developed a greedy folding algorithm (FOLDCAT) with three objectives: optimal \textit{genre category} balance, a user-selectable number of \textit{genre categories}, and sufficient track counts per \textit{genre} for robust machine learning and statistical analysis. To realize these, the FOLDCAT algorithm iteratively processes leaf \textit{genres} beginning with those deepest in the hierarchy and largest in track count, continually moving upwards to the root. For each leaf \textit{genre}, it checks whether a minimum track count threshold is met. This is computed as the sum of vote-weighted assignment probabilities across all tracks corresponding to the leaf.  If it does and its supergenre can sustain removal, the algorithm keeps that leaf \textit{genre} as a \textit{genre category}. Otherwise, the algorithm absorbs the leaf \textit{genre}---along with its siblings and any descendants already processed---into its supergenre by remapping all track assignments upward. This folding procedure continues until all remaining \textit{genres} meet the minimum threshold.  For a desired number of \textit{genre categories}, users may then select the folding solution corresponding to a local Gini minimum, drawing on a hierarchy-level-weighted Gini coefficient (see section~\ref{sec:gini_explanation}), thereby ensuring well-balanced \textit{categories} across all hierarchy levels. 

For final category assignment, each track in the corpus obtains a single \textit{genre label} via majority voting. Thereto, all  original genre tags are mapped to their \textit{category assignment} in folded COMGET, then each track receives the \textit{category} tag with the highest summed votes. Ties are resolved by selecting the \textit{genre category} with lower total assignment probability in the corpus thereby favoring more detailed \textit{genre category} assignments. A detailed flowchart of the FOLDCAT algorithm is provided in the appendix. For the present exemplary analysis using the POPTRAG corpus turned into COMGET-G, we conducted a grid search across minimum \textit{genre sizes} from 1,000 to 15,000 in increments of 5 and then selected four different local Gini minima from the results for comparison.

Notably, the majority of MusicBrainz community members did not classify \textit{heavy metal} tracks as a \textit{subgenre category} of \textit{metal} but rather  as a direct \textit{subgenre category} of \textit{rock} on the same hierarchy level as \textit{metal}.

COMGET-G25 introduces further \textit{rock subgenre categories} (\textit{punk}, \textit{progressive rock}, \textit{folk}, \textit{folk rock}, \textit{classic rock}, \textit{blues rock}, \textit{singer-songwriter}) and additional \textit{pop subgenre categories} (\textit{soul}, \textit{classical}, \textit{synth-pop}, \textit{r\&b}). A third hierarchy level appears with \textit{death metal} as a \textit{subgenre category} of \textit{metal} and \textit{power metal} as a \textit{subgenre category} of \textit{heavy metal}. COMGET-G32 adds \textit{house} as an \textit{electronic subgenre category} and additional subgenre categories in the \textit{rock} and \textit{pop} branches (\textit{blues}, \textit{country}, \textit{new wave}, \textit{soft rock}, \textit{dance-pop}, \textit{reggae}). \textit{hip hop} \textit{subgenre categories} appear only in more fine-grained solutions that we do not document here (\textit{pop rap} as 33rd genre, \textit{trap} as 38th genre).

Both analyses were conducted with different genre granularities.

\subsection{Methods b: Machine Learning Workflow}

In our machine learning workflow, we first discarded tracks with more than 40\% missing values on any variable, retaining 155,093 tracks (92\%). We then performed an 80/20 train–holdout split of all observations on artist level to mitigate possible artist leakage effects~\citep{flexerEffectsAlbumArtist2010}. For training our classifiers, we employed 5-fold cross-validation (also group-split on artist level) for hyperparameter tuning. To reduce sparsity and high-cardinality effects in categorical predictors, we drew a 20\% subsample of the training set, created artist‑grouped CV folds on that subsample, and relabeled any factor level that occurred fewer than 100 times in at least one fold as ``other''. Predictors that collapsed to a single ``other''-level as well as two‑level predictors where the ``other'' level had fewer than 100 observations were removed. This preprocessing reduced the number of variables from 192 to 149. All remaining categorical predictors were dummy-coded, using the most common level as the reference.

Further preprocessing was applied in sequence within each single fold: median imputation, adaptive class balancing (excluded for holdout set), and normalization. Adaptive class balancing employed a single tunable \textit{target-ratio} parameter governing rebalancing degree. The algorithm computed a reference count from outcome class frequencies (median for $\leq$ 10 classes; 60th percentile for 11–20 classes; 65th percentile for $>$ 20 classes), then downsampled classes exceeding reference $\times$ \textit{target ratio} using NearMiss~\citep{zhangKNNApproachUnbalanced2003} and upsampled classes below reference $\div$ \textit{target ratio} using Borderline SMOTE~\citep{hanBorderlineSMOTENewOverSampling2005} or regular SMOTE when sufficient danger observations were unavailable. This approach preserved more data than fixed-ratio sampling while remaining tunable: \textit{target ratio} = 1 enforced perfect balance; higher values retained original imbalance. The reference-count heuristic scaled to problem complexity, preventing excessive downsampling at higher resolutions.

For hyperparameter search in terms of this target-ratio and learner algorithm settings, we employed Bayesian optimization~\citep{snoekPracticalBayesianOptimization2012a} with a Latin hypercube initial grid ~\citep{husslageSpacefillingLatinHypercube2011}, early stopping if no improvement occurred after 10 iterations, and exploration of uncertain hyperparameter regions after 5 iterations without improvement. The final hyperparameters were selected over all tunes using the one-standard-error rule~\citep{hastieModelAssessmentSelection2009}. We tuned hyperparameters across five folds using the macro-averaged F1 score (mean per-class F1) to treat all \textit{genres} equally during lerner evaluation. 

During initial hyperparameter tuning, we evaluated four different supervised ML algorithms based on a 20\% subsample of the training data at low (5) and medium (25) granularity to identify the most suitable learner out of 4 options: elastic net (GLMNET; Tay et al.,~\citeyear{tayetalElasticNet2023}), regularized discriminant analysis (RDA; Weihs et al.,~\citeyear{weihsetalKlaR2005}), random forest (RF; Wright and Ziegler,~\citeyear{wrightetalRanger2017}), and light gradient boosted machine (LightGBM; Shi et al.,~\citeyear{shietalLightgbm2025}). For each we tuned key hyperparameters: GLMNET — \textit{penalty} and \textit{mixture} in [0, 1]; RDA — \textit{covariance fraction} and \textit{identity fraction} in [0, 1]; RF — \textit{feature fraction considered at each split} in [1, \# of predictors] and \textit{leaf node size} in [1, 100]; number of trees was set to 500 for tuning and 1000 for final models; LightGBM — \textit{boosting rounds} in [50, 2000], \textit{learning rate} in [0.001, 0.1], \textit{leaf node size} in [1, 100], \textit{tree depth} in [3, 15], and \textit{feature fraction} in [1, \# of predictors]. For all learners, the target ratio used for adaptive balancing was tuned in between [1, max(obsevations per class)/min(observations per class)]. To identify the best learner, we employed a coarse hyperparameter search with a 10-level initial grid and a maximum of 25 iterations for Bayesian optimization. Higher levels of granularity (25, 32) were not considered at this initial step due to sparseness of outcome levels in the 20\% subsample.

After selecting the learner with best performance accross folds, we performed a more extensive hyperparameter search with a 20-level initial grid and a maximum of 50 iterations with 5-fold crossvalidation on the full training set for all four levels of genre granularity (5, 12, 25, 32), trained the final classifiers on the full training set, and evaluated results on the holdout set. Then we calculated model-based feature importances and plotted confusion matrices for interpreting classification outcomes. Here, \textit{Community certainty} (overall and for single categories) served as a reference point for evaluating final classifier performance, since ML models logically cannot exceed the consistency of the underlying human community assignments~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. For this comparison \textit{certainty} was computed as the mean assignment probability across all MusicBrains votes for each track that had been mapped to the \textit{single-label genre} during the FOLDCAT procedure that built the category systems. All random operations in the machine learning workflow used the seed 42 for reproducibility.

\section{Discussion}

With the present article, we introduce a novel and methodologically transparent procedure for constructing well-balanced hierarchical genre taxonomies and according genre category assignments from listener-community discourse on genres represented in folksonomies on open music databases. Specifically, we make three methodological key contributions: (RQ1) an algorithm to construct a balanced hierarchical genre tree (COMGET) from folksonomy genre tag co-occurrences, (RQ2) a  folding algorithm (FOLDCAT) that generates optimally balanced hierarchical genre categories and final track-assignments at user-specified granularities from such trees. Finally, exemplarily employing a large German popular music corpus (POPTRAG) and MusicBrainz folksonomy votes, we (RQ3) demonstrate that resulting community-based genre assignments are a) comparable in content to existing and frequently-used genre taxonomies (i.e. \textit{Discogs, Deezer}) while exhibiting more desirable features with a German popular music corpus in terms of category balance and category coverage and b) can be learned well with supervised machine learning by drawing on openly available music descriptors only. Hence, we recommend our novel approach  as a transparent, reproducible, and empirically grounded alternative to commercial or expert-curated genre taxonomies in future applied or basic research. The method can in principle be adopted to any other music corpus and other genre tagging systems that provide co-occurences of tags (e.g. \textit{Spotify}, \textit{YouTube}, \textit{SoundCloud}). Also, while we focussed our exemplary analysis on track genre, the approach can also be used for assigning genres to artists or releases in a similar way.


\subsection{Benefits of the presented approach for Computational Musicology and Cultural Analysis}

Our COMGET analysis of the POPTRAG dataset of German popular music (RQ1) illustrates the method's capacity to computationally uncover popular music genre discourse structures . \textit{Rock}, \textit{pop}, and \textit{hip hop} emerged as three primary clusters for German popular music genres in Germany, partially aligning with \citet{silverGenreComplexesPopular2016} who identified three main international clusters analyzing MySpace artist networks in 2007. However, structures differ: \textit{rock} forms a distinct cluster in POPTRAG versus Silver et al.'s scattered "Niche" genres; \textit{hip hop} shows minimal differentiation (consistent with Silver et al.); \textit{pop} exhibits stylistic diversity versus Silver et al.'s "Rock 'n Roll" category. Direct comparison remains limited—Silver analyzed artists on a social networking platform, we analyze tracks in a national listening corpus across different decades—yet the method enables such comparative analyses across corpora, revealing how genre discourse varies by platform, national context, and historical period~\citep{pichlMiningCultureSpecificMusic2017,nieInaccuratePredictionGenre2022}. 

Crucially,  hierarchical COMGET genre taxonomies as obtained in results of RQ1 also capture how contemporary music listeners in general categorize popular music rather than musicological historical genealogies~\citep{pachetTaxonomyMusicalGenres2000} do. While the COMGET-G reveals indeed some unambiguos genealogical links (\textit{rock} $\rightarrow$ \textit{alternative rock}), we also obtained some at-first-glance counterintuitive links that rather invite a popular music discourse interpretation rather than genealogical correction from a musicological stance. For instance, \textit{classical} and \textit{jazz} emerge as \textit{pop} subgenres in the COMGET-G tree. Two factors explain this placement: POPTRAG mostly contains very accessible, popular and partly even crossover  tracks from these genres —popular opera arias, smooth jazz compilations, chart-oriented classical crossover artists. This corpus profile combined with listeners' tendency to apply broad labels when tagging popular music~\citep{bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019} may explain the findings. Similarly, both \textit{heavy metal} and \textit{metal} appear as sibbling \textit{rock} subgenres, reflecting shared historical roots in 1970s psychedelic and hard rock while acknowledging \textit{metal's} later distinctiveness~\citep{gracykHeavyMetalGenre2016}.

Finally, both, COMGET (RQ1) and FOLDCAT (RQ2) consequently implement power-critical concepts from popular music studies and cultural analysis~\citep{dugayDoingCulturalStudies2013} into computational methods for making them applicable with large popular music corpora. Specifically, our approach employs radically democratic principles throughout: community votes determine co-occurrences, strongest links define hierarchies, majority voting assigns single labels. Resulting community genre taxonomies, genre categories, and genre category assignments reflect collective listener discourse rather than industry or musicological expert impositions, democratizing genre knowledge production for computational musicology. We envision that this will improve the validity of music-preference studies by stronger aligning measurements with how listeners actually categorize music.

Music stylistic attributes play a secondary role: They only help to distinguish broad categories of music, then metadata and lyrics appear to be important for differentiation on lower hierarchical levels of genres. \subsection{Benefits of the presented for MIR Research and Recommender Applications}

A core methodological drawback in MIR is the fact that most hitherto genre recognition approaches lack explicit theoretical grounding~\citep{greenCriticalSurveyResearch2024}. Conversely, our approach presented here operationalizes genre-as-discourse~\citep{foucaultArchaeologyKnowledge2013, nealeGenre1980}, treating proper genre assignment as an empirical question rather than an ontological given. Also we theoretically ground the choice of audio features and metadata and lyric features (XXX anonymous author paper). This connects computational methods with popular music studies and enables the MIR community to engage with humanities scholarship regarding the broader topic of cultural categorization~\citep{dimaggioClassificationArt1987}. 

Furthermore, our single-folksonomy approach ensures epistemic consistency. Existing mixed-source methods conflate different communities' genre concepts across platforms with varying demographics, tag voting systems, tag pools, and discursive contexts, introducing uncontrolled variance~\citep{schreiberImprovingGenreAnnotations2015,bogdanovAcousticbrainzGenreDataset2019}. Conversely, single-source taxonomies eliminate cross-platform variance, ensuring all genre assignments represent a specific community's discourse rather than mixing incompatible categorization systems, thereby adressing issues of representativeness in MIR (XXX). By refraining from introducing any arbitrary numeric thresholds throughout the genre categorization pipeline and drawing on open-source implementation, our approach can be simply reproduced and adapted to any music corpus and multi-genre-tag system, as we have demonstrated by using it for constructing industry distribution genres from Spotify tags in RQ3 of this paper. Researchers may replicate the method with their target population's folksonomy, generating taxonomies specific to their chosen listener community.


FOLDCAT addresses diverse practical requirements for handling complex hierarchical genre taxonomies without any unneccessary information loss while enabling cross-study comparison. The folding algorithm generates optimally balanced genre categories at multiple granularities using a single statistical criterion (Gini coefficient) across hierarchy levels. In addition. underlying genre category assignments stay probabilistic. Hence, the framework quantifies achievable performance ceilings through community certainty, since models cannot exceed human consistency~\citep{sturmReviewValidityIts2023}. Obtained large correlations between certainty and classifier performance validate this approach, grounding MIR in listener perception limits. Obtained classifier performance  demonstrates general learnability of the identified genre categories, while systematic confusion patterns reveal fuzzy boundaries (\textit{rock} subgenres), stylistic permeability (\textit{electronic} across \textit{pop}/\textit{jazz}/\textit{indie}), and hierarchical nuance (\textit{heavy metal} versus \textit{metal} asymmetry). These patterns inform which granularities may suit different research questions and reaffirm that genre labels remain learnable in principle by drawing on a theoretically proposed set of features and metadate suggested by the anonymized authors.%Lepa and Radke (2026). 


\subsection{Limitations}
Several constraints limit interpretation and generalization of our exemplary results. For our exemplary analyis with POPTRAG dataset we drew on MusicBrainz community votes. This crowdbased music database was launched in 2000 and does not provide individual vote timestamps, so COMGET-G aggregates roughly 25 years of listener votes spanning a long time period across which the perception of genre boundaries might have most likely shifted gradually.  Furthermore, platform user demographics and expertise remain opaque (approximately 1.2 million anonymous editor accounts; MusicBrainz,~\citeyear{MusicBrainz_nodate}), preventing assessment of a possibly sociodemographic or expertise bias. For the present analysis we had to exclude 36\%  tracks of our POPTRAG dataset due to insufficient genre tags or low number of votes, thereby introducing a substantial popularity bias to our analysis— which is probable acceptable for our popular music focus but possibly problematic for analyses directed at more niche genres. Radical-democratic reduction of multi-tag may have reduced nuances, though this simplification proves necessary for many applied research tasks and aligns with our theory. Methodologically, collinearity among predictors complicates feature importance interpretation. 

\subsection{Future Directions}
Future work will pursue into three different directions. First, we plan to convene German popular music experts to review obtained COMGET-G taxonomy. Thereto, we will label prototypical tracks according to our machine learning with genre categories at different levels of granularity, and identify misassignments to strengthen COMGET-G's validity. Second, we plan to replicate the pipeline on independent folksonomies and regional corpora of tracks (e.g., US and UK charts) to reveal how national listening cultures reshape genre structure. Third, we are aiming at enhancing classfication performance further by employing LLMs to produce advanced lyric features .

Beyond  MIR and computational musicology,  the approach presented may also offer new possibilities benefits for stakeholders across the music industry: radio programmers,  playlist editors, and music librarians to gain insight into how audiences organize music; A\&R teams to identify gaps between industry positioning and listener reception; marketing departments to recognize which subcultural boundaries audiences perceive versus industry-imposed categories; music journalists to ground critical language in empirical listener consensus; and platform designers to align recommendation logic with listeners' mental models.
